It is sometimes suggested that a function f is continuous on a closed interval [a, b] if and only if f is defined at every point of that interval, f is bounded on that interval, and f takes every value between f(a) and f(b). The motivation for this account is not difficult to discern: by the extreme value theorem, continuous functions on closed bounded intervals are bounded, and by the intermediate value theorem, they attain every intermediate value. The definition attempts to reverse these celebrated theorems, taking their conclusions as a characterisation. Yet consider the function defined on [0, 1] by f(x) = sin(1/x) for x in the open interval (0, 1] and f(0) = 0. This function is defined at every point of [0, 1]. It is bounded, since the sine function never exceeds one in absolute value. And because sin(1/x) oscillates with ever-increasing frequency as x approaches 0, the function passes through every value between negative one and one in every neighbourhood of the origin, so it certainly takes every value between f(0) and f(1). All three conditions of the proposed definition are satisfied. But f is manifestly not continuous at 0: as x tends to 0, sin(1/x) does not approach any limit, let alone the value f(0) = 0. The definition has confused the global consequences of continuity with its local essence. A function is continuous at a point because its values near that point approximate its value at that point, and no combination of boundedness and value-attainment can substitute for this fundamental local requirement.

The difficulty is that the definition has elevated stability and longevity to necessary conditions, when in truth ecosystems are defined by their current structure and processes: energy flow, nutrient cycling, and biotic interactions. And it reproduces: sparks and burning embers are lofted by convection currents and carried by the wind to ignite new fires at locations distant from the original, generating fresh instances of the same phenomenon. All three conditions of the definition are satisfied. Yet no biologist would classify a fire as alive. The background cases were all cellular organisms whose metabolism, growth, and reproduction are directed by a heritable programme encoded in nucleic acids — a genome that specifies the processes of self-maintenance and replication. The definition abstracted the outward manifestations of these genome-directed processes without capturing the directive principle that distinguishes biological activity from mere combustion. A fire metabolises, grows, and reproduces, but it does so by the uncontrolled play of chemical forces rather than by the execution of an internally encoded programme.

In each case, the investigator manipulated a variable, compared outcomes under different conditions, and isolated the variable of interest from confounding factors. The capacity to intervene — to hold some things constant while varying others — is what gives these experiments their power to establish causal relationships.

One might attempt to characterise a scientific experiment as an investigation involving the deliberate manipulation of a variable, the comparison of outcomes under different values of that variable, and the isolation of the variable from confounders. This definition captures the interventionist paradigm admirably. Yet a case which reveals its insufficiency arises in the historical and observational sciences. Consider a geological investigation of the formation of the Grand Canyon. The investigator studies the layers of sedimentary rock, analyses patterns of erosion, and examines the chemical composition of different strata. She compares different layers and draws conclusions about the causal processes — river erosion, tectonic uplift, climatic change — that shaped the canyon over millions of years. Her investigation is rigorous, systematic, and yields reliable conclusions. Yet she manipulates no variable, for she cannot intervene in geological time. She compares outcomes not under experimentally controlled conditions but under the naturally occurring variation that geological history provides. None of the three conditions of the definition are met. What the background cases possess and the geological investigation lacks is the capacity for active intervention. Yet the geological investigation achieves a form of rigour that the definition cannot recognise. It appears that the definition, by identifying scientific experiment with interventionist manipulation, excludes rigorous empirical investigations that draw reliable causal conclusions from the comparative study of natural variation.

Suppose one defines a sequence of real numbers to converge to a limit L if and only if for every positive epsilon there exists a natural number N such that for all n greater than N the absolute value of a_n minus L is less than epsilon, the sequence is Cauchy, and the limit L is rational. The first condition is the standard epsilon-N definition of convergence. The second is automatically satisfied by any convergent sequence of real numbers, so it adds no further restriction. The third, however, is a substantive demand: it requires the limit to be a rational number. Consider the sequence a_n = (1 + 1/n)^n. It is well known that this sequence converges; its limit is the base of the natural logarithm, the number e, approximately 2.71828. The convergence satisfies the epsilon-N criterion, and the sequence is Cauchy. But e is irrational — indeed, it is transcendental, as Hermite proved in 1873. The third condition fails. The proposed definition would deny that this sequence converges, which is absurd. The difficulty is that the definition has imposed an extraneous arithmetic restriction upon the limit. Convergence is a topological and metric concept: it concerns the proximity of terms to a limit, not the algebraic character of that limit. A sequence may converge to the square root of two, to pi, to e, or to any other real number, and the definition of convergence must be broad enough to accommodate all such limits without prejudice.

Consider three functions that are paradigmatically continuous. The polynomial function defined by the square of the argument is continuous at every point of the real line. It is defined everywhere; for every sequence converging to a given point, the images converge to the square of that point; and it maps bounded sets to bounded sets. The exponential function is continuous at every point. It is defined everywhere; it preserves the convergence of sequences; and it maps bounded sets to bounded sets. The sine function is continuous at every point. It is defined everywhere; it preserves sequential convergence; and it is itself bounded, so it trivially maps bounded sets to bounded sets. From these cases one might extract a definition: a function is continuous at a point if and only if it is defined there, it preserves the convergence of sequences to that point, and it maps bounded sets to bounded sets in every neighbourhood of the point.

The first two conditions constitute the well-known sequential characterisation of continuity, which is equivalent to the standard epsilon-delta definition and is universally accepted as correct. The third condition — that the function maps bounded sets to bounded sets in neighbourhoods of the point — is satisfied by all three background cases and might therefore seem an independent necessary condition. Yet it is not independent. It is a consequence of the sequential characterisation. If a function preserves the convergence of every sequence converging to a point, then it is bounded on every sufficiently small neighbourhood of that point, and hence it maps bounded subsets of such neighbourhoods to bounded sets. The third condition adds no content that is not already entailed by the second. The background cases were all well-behaved functions that exhibited many pleasant properties beyond mere continuity, and the definition has extracted one such property — boundedness preservation — and elevated it to the status of an independent condition, when in truth it follows from the sequential criterion as a logical consequence. The analysis has mistaken a theorem about continuous functions for an independent axiom, inflating the definition with a redundant condition that creates an appearance of additional rigour without adding any genuine content.

One might observe that this analysis accounts for many familiar cases: the custom of shaking hands upon greeting, of saying grace before meals, or of dressing in a particular manner for particular occasions. Yet we should hesitate to accept the majority condition as necessary. Consider a society in which the practice of removing one's shoes before entering a home is observed by only a minority of households — perhaps thirty per cent. Within these households, the practice is transmitted through social learning: children acquire it by observation and imitation, not through formal instruction. And deviation from it is met with genuine disapproval: a guest who wears shoes into such a home is regarded as having committed a social fault. All the marks of custom are present except majority observance. Yet those who practise shoe-removal speak of it as a custom, expect compliance, and feel a sense of normative violation when it is disregarded. This case reveals the insufficiency of the analysis, for it shows that a custom may be sustained by a sub-group and derive its force from the expectations of that group.

From these cases one might extract a definition: a person has a moral obligation to perform an action if and only if he has voluntarily entered into a role carrying the relevant duty, was aware at the time of entering the role that the duty attached to it, and has the competence and resources to perform the action.

The explanation satisfies every stated condition — deducibility, confirmation, truth of initial conditions — yet cites factors that played no causal role. One might observe that what the background cases share, and what the definition fails to capture, is the requirement that the cited factors be causally responsible for the phenomenon rather than merely co-occurring conditions from which it can be logically derived.

Yet the line segment joining two diametrically opposite points on the circle — say the points (1, 0) and (-1, 0) — passes through the origin and through the interior of the circle, which are not part of the circle itself. The second condition fails. The proposed definition has confused connectedness with convexity, imposing the requirement that straight-line segments between points remain in the set, when connectedness demands only that the set cannot be split into two non-empty separated parts. A circle, an annulus, a spiral, and countless other connected sets are not convex, and any characterisation that requires convexity will exclude them.

Three cases suggest a common structure of causation. A billiard ball strikes a stationary ball and sets it in motion: the collision precedes the movement, a physical mechanism — the transfer of momentum — connects them, and without the collision the stationary ball would have remained at rest. A drug reduces a fever: its administration precedes the change, its pharmacological action provides the mechanism, and without it the fever would have persisted. The definition captured the counterfactual dependence that held in these clean scenarios but could not accommodate the common situation in which multiple sufficient causes compete and one pre-empts the other.

This formulation captures the differentiating, transitional, and comprehensive requirements that we associate with useful historical periodisation. Yet a case which reveals the insufficiency of this proposal may be drawn from the periodisation of European economic history. Suppose one divides European history into an Age of Agriculture, extending to roughly 1750; an Age of Industry, from 1750 to 1950; and an Age of Information, from 1950 to the present. Each period is characterised by a distinctive dominant mode of economic production. The transitions correspond to significant technological shifts: the mechanisation of production and the subsequent rise of computing. The periodisation covers the entire span of European history without gaps or overlaps. All three conditions of the proposed definition are satisfied. Nevertheless, one might observe that this periodisation imposes a false uniformity upon a process that was in reality profoundly uneven. Large regions of southern and eastern Europe remained predominantly agricultural well into the twentieth century. The transition to industrial production occurred decades or even centuries later in some countries than in others. The neat boundary at 1750 reflects the experience of England far more than that of Spain or Romania. It appears that the definition, by requiring only distinctive features, significant transitions, and temporal completeness, fails to demand that the periodisation reflect the actual distribution and timing of the characterising features across the relevant domain. Without such a requirement, the periodisation may impose a spurious uniformity that obscures more than it reveals.

The most instructive experiments in the history of natural science share a structural feature that the most straightforward definitions of decisive testing fail to articulate. When Pasteur designed his swan-neck flask experiment, its persuasiveness rested on the fact that the only difference between the experimental and control conditions was whether airborne particles could reach the broth; every other factor was held constant. When Lavoisier heated mercury in a sealed vessel, the sealed system ensured that any change in mass could be attributed solely to a transfer between the mercury and the enclosed air. When Joule measured the mechanical equivalent of heat, the insulation of his apparatus ensured that the only pathway for the water's temperature to rise was through the mechanical work performed.

These cases invite a general characterisation: a decisive test manipulates exactly one variable while holding all others constant, measures the outcome with sufficient precision, and is repeated enough times to exclude chance. Yet a case which reveals the insufficiency of this formulation may be constructed from agricultural biology. Suppose a biologist tests the hypothesis that a particular fertiliser increases plant growth by applying the fertiliser to one set of plots while leaving a control set untreated. She manipulates only the fertiliser variable, holds all other conditions — soil type, sunlight, water — constant, measures growth with great precision, and repeats the experiment many times with consistent results. All three conditions are met. Yet suppose the fertiliser contains a trace contaminant, a heavy metal that also promotes growth by a distinct mechanism. The observed increase in growth is produced partly by the fertiliser and partly by the contaminant, and the experiment cannot disentangle these causal contributions. What the background cases reveal — and what the definition fails to require — is that the manipulation must be pure: that the intervention alter only the factor specified by the hypothesis, introducing no additional confounded causal influences.

Reflection upon several familiar instances of composite numbers might lead one to formulate a general characterisation of compositeness. Fifteen is composite because it admits the factorisation three times five; twenty-one, because it equals three times seven; thirty-five, because it equals five times seven. In each case, the number can be expressed as a product of two factors each strictly between one and the number itself. One observes, moreover, that each of these numbers is odd and greater than two.

These cases invite a general characterisation: a positive integer greater than one is composite if it can be expressed as such a product, is greater than two, and is odd. Yet a case which reveals the insufficiency of this formulation is readily at hand. Consider the number four. It can be written as two times two, both factors lying strictly between one and four, so condition (i) is met. It is greater than two, so condition (ii) is met. But four is even, and condition (iii) requires oddness. The definition therefore classifies four as not composite — that is, as prime — which is plainly false. The same difficulty arises for every even composite number: six, eight, ten, twelve, and so on. One might observe that the background cases from which the definition was induced all happened to involve odd numbers, and the condition of oddness was abstracted from that accidental feature of the sample rather than from any genuine requirement of compositeness. The correct analysis of compositeness demands only that the number admit a non-trivial factorisation; it imposes no constraint upon the number's parity. This instance illustrates a general hazard of inductive generalisation: a condition that is present in every observed case may be an artefact of the sample rather than a feature of the concept.

One might propose that a political arrangement constitutes a social contract if and only if it was established by an explicit act of collective agreement, its terms are recorded in a written document accessible to all, and every member of the society voluntarily consented at the time of its establishment. The definition has a certain appeal when one considers the founding of the American republic, where delegates met in convention, debated and drafted a written constitution, and the document was submitted to the states for ratification. One might suppose that explicit agreement, written terms, and voluntary consent are the essential elements of any social contract. Yet one must attend to the English constitution. Yet the English constitution embodies a social contract in a perfectly intelligible sense — an understanding, implicit and evolving, between the crown and the people, between Parliament and the electorate, regarding the terms upon which political authority is exercised and the limits that constrain it. The definition, in requiring explicit agreement, a written document, and universal consent, captures the features of one particular model of constitutional founding and mistakes them for features that any social contract must possess.

The definition has captured one way in which cooperative arrangements may arise — through deliberate agreement — but has failed to recognise that they may also emerge through the gradual, unplanned evolution of social practice.

From these cases one might extract a definition: a person acts virtuously if and only if the person deliberates about the right course, chooses that course on the basis of a correct moral judgement, and acts from a sense of duty rather than from inclination.

One might attempt to characterise a genuine paradox by three conditions: that it present an argument with apparently true premises and apparently valid steps leading to an apparently false conclusion, that it have resisted resolution by experts for a sustained period, and that it arise within a well-defined formal framework. The first condition captures the essential structure of paradox: the collision between plausible premises and an implausible conclusion. The second is intended to distinguish genuine paradoxes from superficial puzzles that admit of ready resolution. The third restricts the notion to the domain of formal logic or mathematics. Yet we should hesitate to accept the third condition. Consider the ancient paradox of the heap. One grain of sand, we are inclined to say, is not a heap. And if a collection of grains is not a heap, then adding a single grain does not transform it into one. These premises appear plainly true. The step from each case to the next appears valid. Yet the conclusion — that no quantity of sand constitutes a heap — is manifestly false. The paradox has resisted satisfactory resolution for over two thousand years, and distinguished philosophers continue to disagree about its treatment. The first two conditions are met. But the third is not: the paradox is stated in the imprecise vocabulary of ordinary language, not within any well-defined formal framework. It is, nevertheless, among the most celebrated and instructive of all paradoxes. We are led to conclude that the analysis is too restrictive in requiring a formal setting and must be broadened.

A case which reveals the insufficiency of this analysis is furnished by Galileo's celebrated thought experiment concerning the rate of fall of heavy and light bodies. If these background assumptions are false, as they would be in a medium where viscosity is significant, the conclusion of the thought experiment would be mistaken. The definition therefore fails to ensure that the a priori status of the thought experiment is not undermined by the empirical character of its tacit premisses.

Consider the argument: premiss one, 'It is raining'; premiss two, 'It is not raining'; conclusion, 'The moon is made of green cheese.' The premisses are manifestly inconsistent. The argument is valid — trivially so, by explosion. The first condition fails. Likewise, the argument 'P; therefore P' has its conclusion contained in its premisses, yet it is valid. The second condition fails. The definition has imported conditions — consistency and non-circularity — that are desiderata for good reasoning but not necessary conditions of validity as such.

Consider three cases of self-deception. A woman whose husband is plainly unfaithful insists, against all evidence, that he is devoted to her; we say she is self-deceived. A gambler who has lost a fortune persists in the conviction that his luck is about to turn, despite overwhelming statistical evidence to the contrary; he too is self-deceived. A man diagnosed with a serious illness insists he is perfectly well, ignoring the testimony of multiple physicians; again, self-deception. From these cases one might extract a definition: a person is self-deceived about p if and only if p is false, the person holds p to be true, and the person has access to sufficient evidence that p is false.

Yet the analysis encounters a subtle difficulty. Suppose a man holds the proposition that his colleagues like him. He has access to what appears to be evidence to the contrary: his colleagues whisper behind his back, greet him coolly, and exclude him from social gatherings. Yet in fact his colleagues do like him; the apparent evidence of hostility arises from their natural reserve and cultural reticence. The proposition p — that his colleagues like him — is true. The first condition of the definition fails. Yet the man's grounds for holding p are not the evidence at all; he holds p because of a desperate need to be liked, and he would hold p just as tenaciously if his colleagues truly despised him. His belief-forming process is precisely that of the self-deceived person: he ignores apparent counter-evidence and clings to a comforting proposition for motivational reasons. The background cases all involved false propositions, and the definition required falsity. But self-deception, one suspects, is better characterised by the irrational, motivationally-driven process of belief-formation than by the truth-value of the resulting proposition.

One might characterise an adaptation as a heritable trait that increases an organism's fitness in the face of an environmental challenge and that confers higher reproductive success upon those who possess it. This formulation captures the fitness-enhancing, genetic, and differential-success features associated with adaptive traits. Yet a case which reveals the insufficiency of this analysis may be drawn from population genetics. Suppose a mutation arises spontaneously in a population of moths, causing their wings to become transparent rather than bearing the usual opaque colouration. In the moths' environment, predatory birds locate their prey by detecting the dark silhouettes of moths resting on pale tree bark. The transparent-winged moths, presenting no silhouette, happen to escape predation at a much higher rate. The trait increases the probability of survival when predation pressure is present. It is genetically based and heritable: the mutation is passed from parent to offspring. Moths with transparent wings enjoy higher reproductive success than their opaque-winged counterparts. All three conditions of the proposed definition are satisfied. Yet the mutation arose only a single generation ago, entirely by chance. It has not been subject to natural selection over time; there has been no process of differential reproduction extending across generations. Biologists would describe this as a fortuitous variation, not an adaptation. It appears that the definition, by requiring only present fitness advantage, heritability, and differential success, fails to demand that the trait have a history of selection. One is led to conclude that adaptation is an essentially historical concept, requiring that the trait have been shaped or maintained by selection over successive generations in response to the relevant environmental challenge.

A straightforward definition of the gene might run as follows: a segment of DNA constitutes a gene if and only if it encodes the information necessary for the synthesis of a protein, it is heritable, and it occupies a specific, fixed locus on a chromosome. This definition has a pleasing clarity and reflects the central dogma of molecular biology — that DNA encodes protein through the intermediary of messenger RNA. A gene for haemoglobin, for instance, is a segment of DNA that encodes the amino acid sequence of the haemoglobin protein, is transmitted from parent to child, and sits at a definite position on a particular chromosome. The definition correctly identifies this segment as a gene and excludes non-functional stretches of DNA that encode no protein and have no heritable effect. Yet one must consider the genes that encode ribosomal RNA. The ribosome, which is the cellular machinery responsible for translating messenger RNA into protein, is itself composed in large part of RNA molecules. These RNA molecules are encoded by specific DNA segments that are transcribed — that is, copied into RNA — but never translated into protein. They are the final product; the RNA itself, not any protein, is what the cell requires. The segments of DNA that encode ribosomal RNA are heritable, for they are passed from parent to offspring with high fidelity. They occupy specific loci on the chromosomes. But they do not encode the information necessary for the synthesis of a protein. They encode RNA. The first condition of the definition fails. Yet these segments are among the most important genes in any organism — indeed, they are among the most conserved sequences across the whole of life, testimony to their indispensable function. No geneticist would deny that they are genes. The definition has taken one important function of genes — protein encoding — and mistaken it for the only function, overlooking the large and biologically crucial category of genes whose product is RNA rather than protein.

One might propose that a statement is a lie if and only if the speaker knows it to be false, makes it with the intention of causing the hearer to form a false belief, and benefits materially from the hearer's forming that belief. The first two conditions capture the deceptive core of lying — the conjunction of known falsehood and intention to mislead. The third condition adds a motive: the liar lies for gain. This addition is intended to distinguish malicious lies from innocent or inconsequential falsehoods. Yet one might observe that the material-benefit condition is far too restrictive. Consider a parent who tells a young child on Christmas morning that Father Christmas delivered the presents under the tree. The parent knows the statement is false. The parent intends the child to believe it — indeed, the parent goes to some trouble to maintain the fiction, setting out biscuits and milk the night before and carefully disguising the handwriting on the gift tags. But the parent derives no material benefit whatsoever from the child's belief. The motive is sentimental and cultural, not mercenary. The third condition fails, yet the statement is plainly a lie — a kindly one, embedded in a beloved tradition, but a falsehood knowingly told with the intention to deceive. The definition has confused the motive that accompanies some lies — the desire for material gain — with a necessary condition of lying as such. Lies may be told for gain, for kindness, for convenience, or for no discernible reason at all; what makes them lies is not the motive but the knowing utterance of falsehood with intent to deceive.

Process reliabilism offers an elegantly naturalistic account of epistemic justification, holding that a belief is justified just in case it is the product of a reliable belief-forming process — one which yields a suitably high proportion of true beliefs. On this view, it matters not whether the subject is aware of the process or can articulate reasons in its favour; what matters is the track record, as it were, of the cognitive mechanism responsible for the belief. One might initially find this analysis entirely satisfactory, for it accords well with our ordinary practice of distinguishing between reliable and unreliable methods of inquiry. Yet a case which reveals the insufficiency of this analysis may be constructed. Imagine a man who, owing to a neurological abnormality, possesses a lesion in his brain. This lesion, by a remarkable coincidence of causal pathways, invariably produces in him the belief that he has a brain lesion. The process is, in a straightforward sense, perfectly reliable: every time it operates, it produces a true belief. The subject has never entertained this belief for any other reason; the lesion is the sole cause. Nevertheless, one would hesitate to pronounce this belief justified. The process, though reliable, is not of the sort we should recognise as an epistemically appropriate method of belief formation. It is, one might say, an accident of pathology rather than an exercise of cognitive virtue. The reliabilist analysis, therefore, appears to require supplementation with a condition specifying that the process must be of an epistemically respectable type.

The deontological conception of justification draws an analogy between epistemic and moral obligation: just as the moral agent has duties to act rightly, so the epistemic agent has duties to seek truth and avoid error. On this analysis, a belief is justified when the subject has fulfilled her epistemic duties — when she has investigated with due diligence, weighed the evidence impartially, and formed her belief in accordance with her obligations as a seeker of truth. The fulfilment of these duties is held to be sufficient for justification. One might observe that this account captures an important feature of our practice of epistemic appraisal, for we do, in common life, evaluate believers in terms of their intellectual conscientiousness. Yet a case which reveals the insufficiency of this analysis may be drawn from the history of science. Consider a natural philosopher of the 17th century who sets out to understand the nature of combustion. He observes with the utmost care, reasons with diligence and rigour, and consults the most authoritative sources available to him. He fulfils every epistemic duty that could be imposed upon a responsible inquirer of his period. On the basis of this conscientious inquiry, he forms the belief that combustion involves the release of a substance called phlogiston. His duties are discharged; he has done all that could reasonably be asked. Yet his belief is false, and from the vantage of later chemistry it appears unjustified, for the phlogiston theory is fundamentally mistaken. The difficulty is that his epistemic duties were calibrated to the conceptual and evidential resources of his era, which were not adequate for arriving at the truth about combustion. The analysis must, it appears, require that the duties in question be tied to resources sufficient for truth-attainment, not merely to the best available practices of a given historical moment.

Consider three cases of reparation. A man who has broken his neighbour's fence repairs it at his own expense: the damage is undone, and the victim is restored to his prior condition. A thief who has stolen money returns it with interest: the financial loss is made good. A publisher who has printed a libel issues a retraction: the harm to reputation is partly repaired. From these cases one might define adequate reparation: an act of reparation is adequate if and only if it restores the victim to the condition he occupied before the wrong, it is performed voluntarily by the wrongdoer, and the victim accepts it as sufficient.

But this definition, drawn from cases in which the harm is reversible, cannot accommodate wrongs that leave permanent damage. Consider a surgeon whose negligence during an operation leaves a patient permanently disabled — unable to walk, unable to work, unable to live as he lived before. No act of reparation can restore this patient to his pre-injury condition. The damage is irreversible; the first condition fails and can never be satisfied. Yet reparation is still owed. Financial compensation, though it cannot undo the disability, is a genuine form of reparation — it acknowledges the wrong, it provides the means for the patient to adjust to his altered circumstances, and it expresses a recognition of the wrongdoer's responsibility. The background cases all involved harms that could be fully reversed, and the definition abstracted full restoration as the standard of adequacy. But for permanent and irreversible injuries, reparation must be reconceived as acknowledgement and mitigation rather than as restoration.

One might propose that a system is a language if and only if it can be used to make true or false statements about the world, it is used for the communication of factual information, and it is translatable into every other language without loss of meaning. The third condition reflects a tempting assumption: that all languages, being systems for expressing truths about a common world, must be mutually intertranslatable — that anything sayable in one language is sayable in every other. Yet one must consider languages whose vocabularies carve the world in ways radically different from one's own. There exist languages with extensive and finely differentiated kinship terminologies — dozens of distinct terms for relations that English designates with a single word, terms for specific degrees of cousinship, for particular kinds of in-law relationships, for connections through maternal or paternal lines that English simply does not distinguish. A speaker of such a language can make precise statements about kinship that cannot be translated into English without lengthy circumlocution, and even then the translation may fail to capture the full import of the original, for the kinship concepts are embedded in a social structure that has no exact analogue in English-speaking societies. The third condition of the definition fails: the language is not translatable into English without loss of meaning. Yet it is indisputably a language. The definition has assumed a degree of commensurability between languages that does not in fact obtain, and it has mistaken an idealised property — perfect translatability — for a necessary condition of language itself.

It is commonly held that lying consists in the assertion of a falsehood believed by the speaker to be false, made with the intention that the hearer should accept it as true. This three-part analysis seems to capture the essence of lying: it excludes honest mistakes, insincere assertions made without deceptive intent, and truths delivered in good faith. Yet a case which tells against the sufficiency of this analysis, or more precisely against the necessity of its first condition, may be constructed from medical practice. Suppose that a physician, wishing to comfort a dying patient, tells him that his condition is improving. The physician believes this statement to be false — she has examined the test results and concluded that the patient's health is declining. She intends the patient to believe her reassuring words. By every intuitive measure, the physician has lied. Yet suppose that, by a remarkable coincidence, new laboratory results arrive moments later revealing that the patient's condition has in fact undergone a dramatic and wholly unexpected improvement. The statement the physician made turns out to be true. The first condition of the proposed definition is not met, since the statement is not false. Yet we should not wish to say that the physician did not lie; her utterance bore every hallmark of deception — she asserted what she believed to be false with the express purpose of misleading her hearer. The definition, by insisting upon actual falsity, mistakes an accidental feature of paradigmatic lies for an essential one and thereby fails to classify as lies those assertions that are intended to deceive but happen, by coincidence, to be true.

One might propose that a set of propositions is consistent if and only if no contradiction can be derived from it, it has at least one model, and every proper subset of it is also consistent. The first two conditions, taken together, constitute the standard account — they are, in the presence of a complete proof system, equivalent. The third condition is novel and merits examination. It might be motivated by the thought that genuine consistency ought to be robust — that a set ought to be consistent not merely as a whole but in every part. This is a species of structural requirement: the consistency of the whole should be grounded in the consistency of each portion. Yet the condition is redundant. If a set S is consistent, then every proper subset of S is necessarily consistent; one cannot derive a contradiction from fewer premisses if one cannot derive a contradiction from more premisses (since anything derivable from a subset is derivable from the whole). The third condition follows logically from the first and, whilst true of every consistent set, adds nothing to the characterisation. It is satisfied trivially. More interestingly, it can be satisfied by inconsistent sets as well: the set containing P, Q, and 'P implies not-Q' is inconsistent, yet every two-element proper subset is consistent. The three conditions are thus not jointly sufficient for consistency, because the third condition does not prevent the kind of inconsistency that arises only when all members of the set are taken together. The definition is flawed in its attempt to strengthen the standard account with a structural requirement that is simultaneously redundant and insufficient.

One might define an ecosystem as a collection of organisms and their physical environment in which the organisms interact through feeding relationships, exchange energy and matter with the environment, and the whole is self-sustaining over ecologically relevant timescales. This definition captures the features of the paradigmatic ecosystems — a temperate forest, a coral reef, a freshwater lake — in which a stable community of producers, consumers, and decomposers cycles nutrients and energy through a persistent physical setting. Yet a case which reveals the insufficiency of this definition is provided by hydrothermal vent communities. At the mid-ocean ridges, superheated water laden with minerals issues from fissures in the sea floor. Around these vents, dense communities of organisms flourish: chemosynthetic bacteria that derive energy from hydrogen sulphide, tube worms that harbour these bacteria, crabs and shrimp that graze upon microbial mats, and various predators. The organisms interact through feeding relationships and exchange energy and matter with the vent environment. But the vents themselves are temporary geological features. A given vent may be active for decades or a few centuries before the flow of mineral-rich water ceases, at which point the community collapses. The collection is not self-sustaining over ecologically relevant timescales; it is entirely dependent upon a transient geological process. The definition excludes it from the category of ecosystem. Yet ecologists regard vent communities as ecosystems without hesitation, and they are studied as such in every major textbook of marine ecology. One might observe that the definition, by requiring self-sustainability, has been constructed from the model of stable, persistent systems and cannot accommodate the transient but ecologically genuine communities that depend upon impermanent energy sources.

One might define civilisational decline as a period in which political institutions are losing their capacity to maintain order, cultural production is diminishing in quality and originality, and material prosperity is decreasing. This definition has the merit of combining the political, cultural, and material dimensions of civilisational health. Yet a case which reveals its insufficiency is furnished by the Eastern Roman Empire in the sixth century. Under Justinian, the empire was beset by formidable difficulties: the wars of reconquest in Italy and North Africa strained the treasury and the army, and the great plague of 542 devastated the population and the economy. By the criteria of political stability and material prosperity, the empire was manifestly declining. Yet this was simultaneously the period of some of the most remarkable cultural achievements in the empire's thousand-year history. Hagia Sophia, completed in 537, represents one of the supreme accomplishments of human architecture. The Corpus Juris Civilis, Justinian's codification of Roman law, has shaped Western legal thought for fifteen centuries. The mosaics of Ravenna display an artistic ambition and a technical mastery that stand comparison with the finest work of any era. One might observe that the definition, by requiring all three dimensions to decline simultaneously, cannot accommodate the evident fact that political decay and cultural flourishing may coexist.

One might maintain that the meaning of a complex expression is determined by the meanings of its parts and the syntactic rules governing their combination. This principle of compositionality has enormous explanatory power: it accounts for the productivity of language — our capacity to understand sentences we have never encountered before — and for its systematicity — the fact that the ability to understand one sentence confers the ability to understand structurally related sentences. Yet a case which reveals the insufficiency of a strict compositional account may be drawn from the phenomenon of idiomatic expressions. Consider the English phrase 'kick the bucket.' The sub-expressions are familiar: 'kick' means to strike with the foot, 'the' is a definite article, and 'bucket' means a cylindrical container. The syntactic structure is a simple verb phrase: verb, determiner, noun. A speaker who grasps the meanings of the sub-expressions and the syntax of the construction will derive the compositional meaning: to strike a cylindrical container with one's foot. But this is not what the expression means. It means to die. The idiomatic meaning is not derivable from the parts; it is conventionally associated with the phrase as a whole. One might observe that idiomatic expressions are not a marginal phenomenon in natural language but a pervasive one. Every language contains thousands of idioms whose meanings cannot be computed from their constituents. This does not refute the principle of compositionality as a general tendency of language, but it does reveal the insufficiency of any definition that treats compositionality as exceptionless. The definition, by asserting that meaning is wholly determined by parts and syntax, cannot accommodate a class of expressions that are among the most commonly used in everyday speech.

It has long been supposed that beauty may be captured by an enumeration of its necessary marks: formal harmony, disinterested pleasure, and a certain vividness of sensory presentation. One might observe that a great many objects we are disposed to call beautiful do indeed satisfy these conditions jointly. A well-proportioned facade, a melody whose intervals resolve with apparent inevitability, a landscape suffused with colour — each exhibits harmony, occasions pleasure untainted by appetite, and strikes the senses with force. Yet we should hesitate to regard this enumeration as sufficient. Suppose that one encounters, along the margin of an estuary, a procession of smooth grey stones, arranged by the tides into a pattern of gentle curvature. The arrangement is formally harmonious; the eye traces its arc with satisfaction. The pleasure one derives is disinterested, for one does not wish to own or consume the stones. And the surfaces, dampened by the retreating water, possess a vivid gleam. Nevertheless, the experience is not aptly described as an apprehension of beauty. It is, rather, an apprehension of something solemn, austere, even mournful. The conditions are met, yet the concept of beauty appears to outrun them. We are led to conclude that the analysis omits a crucial requirement concerning the positive, approving quality of the pleasure elicited.

Suppose one defines a legal person as an entity that is a living human being, possesses the capacity for rational thought, and has attained the age of majority. The definition has a certain intuitive appeal, for the paradigmatic legal person is indeed an adult human being of sound mind who acts in the world, enters into agreements, and bears responsibility for his conduct. Yet the definition is far too narrow. Consider, in the first instance, a joint-stock company duly incorporated under the relevant Companies Act. Such a company is emphatically not a living human being; it is an artificial creation of the law, brought into existence by the filing of certain documents and the payment of certain fees. It possesses no capacity for rational thought, having no mind at all in the ordinary sense of the term. And the notion of an age of majority is plainly inapplicable to it. Yet the law recognises it as a legal person in the fullest sense: it may own property, enter into contracts, incur debts, sue and be sued, and even be convicted of certain offences. Consider further the case of an infant. An infant of three months is a living human being but possesses no capacity for rational thought and has not attained the age of majority. Yet the infant is a legal person: it may inherit property, it may be the beneficiary of a trust, and wrongs done to it are actionable. The definition excludes both corporations and infants from legal personality, and in so doing it confuses the question of who may act in law with full capacity with the quite different question of who is recognised by the law as bearing rights and obligations at all.
