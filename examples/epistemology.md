## DefCx Examples

Each example presents a definition as necessary and sufficient conditions, then constructs a scenario that reveals the definition's insufficiency.

### 1. knowledge

**Definition:** A subject S knows that p if and only if (i) p is true, (ii) S believes that p, and (iii) S is justified in believing that p.

> One of the most venerable analyses in the theory of knowledge holds that knowledge is nothing other than justified true belief. The proposal may be stated thus: a subject S knows that p if and only if (i) p is true, (ii) S believes that p, and (iii) S is justified in believing that p. The appeal of this tripartite analysis is considerable, for each condition appears individually necessary and the conjunction appears sufficient. Truth is plainly required, since one cannot properly be said to know what is false; belief is required, since knowledge is at least a species of assent; and justification is required to distinguish knowledge from mere lucky guessing. Yet one might observe a case which reveals the insufficiency of this analysis. Suppose a man walking through the town square glances at the large clock mounted upon the post office and reads the time as two o'clock. He thereupon forms the belief that it is two o'clock. The clock has, however, been stopped for exactly twelve hours, having broken at two o'clock the previous morning. By a remarkable coincidence, the man happens to consult the clock at precisely two o'clock in the afternoon, so that his belief is true. Moreover, he is justified in this belief, for he has no reason to suspect the clock is broken, and one is ordinarily entitled to trust the accuracy of public timepieces. Nevertheless, it appears that we should deny that the man knows it is two o'clock, for his being right is a matter of pure accident. The case suggests that justified true belief, whilst perhaps necessary for knowledge, is not sufficient.

*Missing condition:* The analysis omits any requirement that the justification be appropriately connected to the fact which makes the belief true, thereby permitting cases of epistemic luck to satisfy all three conditions.

### 2. knowledge

**Definition:** A subject S knows that p if and only if (i) p is true, (ii) S believes that p, (iii) S is justified in believing that p, and (iv) S's justification does not essentially depend upon any false lemma.

> It has been proposed that one may rescue the classical analysis of knowledge by supplementing it with a fourth condition, namely that the subject's justification must not rest upon any false lemma. The analysis thus runs: S knows that p if and only if (i) p is true, (ii) S believes that p, (iii) S is justified in believing that p, and (iv) S's justification does not essentially depend upon any false proposition. The motivation for this addition is clear enough, for many of the troublesome cases in the literature involve a subject who reasons correctly from a false premiss to a conclusion which happens, by good fortune, to be true. Yet one might construct a case which reveals the insufficiency of this analysis in a different direction. Suppose a man, whom we shall call Jones, believes that his colleague Smith owns a Ford motorcar, having observed Smith driving a Ford to the office every morning for the past year. From this belief Jones deduces that at least one person in the office owns a Ford. Now it transpires that Smith's Ford was destroyed in an accident a week ago, and Smith has since been driving a hired vehicle. However, entirely unknown to Jones, another colleague, Brown, has just that morning purchased a Ford. Jones's belief that someone in the office owns a Ford is true, and his justification proceeds through the false lemma that Smith owns a Ford. The no-false-lemma condition correctly excludes this case. Yet one might observe that the condition is perhaps too stringent, for there appear to be instances where a minor falsehood in one's reasoning does not genuinely undermine the epistemic credentials of one's belief, and one would still wish to ascribe knowledge despite the presence of such an inessential falsity in the inferential chain.

*Missing condition:* The no-false-lemma requirement may be too restrictive, for there exist cases in which a belief is justified through a false intermediary yet still constitutes knowledge, provided the falsehood is inessential to the broader evidential support.

### 3. knowledge

**Definition:** A subject S knows that p if and only if (i) p is true, (ii) S believes that p, and (iii) S's belief that p is justified by evidence which, taken together, entails or renders highly probable that p.

> Consider an analysis which holds that knowledge is true belief supported by evidence of sufficient strength. More precisely, S knows that p if and only if (i) p is true, (ii) S believes that p, and (iii) S's belief that p is justified by evidence which, taken together, entails or renders highly probable that p. One might think that the evidential condition is strong enough to exclude cases of epistemic fortune, since the evidence must render the proposition highly probable. Yet a case may be constructed which reveals the insufficiency of this analysis. Suppose a farmer, walking along a country lane, looks across a neighbouring field and observes what she takes to be a sheep grazing near a hedge. She thereupon forms the belief that there is a sheep in the field. Her visual experience is perfectly clear, the light is good, and she is an experienced farmer well acquainted with the appearance of sheep; her evidence, one might say, renders it highly probable that there is a sheep in the field. However, it transpires that the object she is looking at is not a sheep at all but rather a large dog which has been dressed in a fleece by a mischievous child. Yet behind a hillock, quite out of the farmer's line of sight, there does in fact stand a genuine sheep. The farmer's belief is thus true, and her evidence is of the sort that would ordinarily suffice for knowledge, yet it appears that she does not know there is a sheep in the field. The difficulty is that her evidence is connected to the wrong object entirely, and the truth of her belief is secured by a fact of which she is wholly unaware.

*Missing condition:* The analysis fails to require that the evidence upon which the subject relies be connected in the right way to the fact which renders the belief true, rather than to some misleading surrogate.

### 4. knowledge

**Definition:** A subject S has knowledge of a thing X if and only if (i) S is directly acquainted with X, (ii) S's awareness of X is not mediated by any description or inference, and (iii) X is presented to S in immediate experience.

> There is a distinguished tradition in epistemology which draws a sharp line between knowledge by acquaintance and knowledge by description. On this view, the paradigmatic form of knowledge is that in which the subject stands in a direct, unmediated cognitive relation to the object known. We may formalise this as follows: S has knowledge of a thing X if and only if (i) S is directly acquainted with X, (ii) S's awareness of X is not mediated by any description or inference, and (iii) X is presented to S in immediate experience. The thought is that one knows one's own sense-data, one's present feelings of pain or pleasure, and perhaps certain abstract objects such as universals, in a manner which is fundamentally different from the way in which one knows, say, the atomic weight of carbon or the population of India. Yet one might observe a case which reveals the insufficiency of this analysis as an account of knowledge in general. Consider a physicist who has spent her career studying the interior of the sun. She knows that the core temperature is approximately fifteen million degrees, that hydrogen is fused into helium under enormous pressure, and that certain neutrino emissions confirm her theoretical models. She has never been directly acquainted with the sun's interior, nor could she be, and every item of her knowledge is mediated by complex chains of inference, instrumental readings, and the descriptions furnished by physical theory. Yet it would be exceedingly odd to say that she does not know these things. Her knowledge is as well established as any in natural science. The case reveals that knowledge by description, far from being an inferior species of cognition, can attain a degree of certainty and systematicity which rivals or even surpasses what is available through direct acquaintance.

*Missing condition:* The analysis neglects the possibility that knowledge may be attained through well-grounded descriptions and inferences, even when direct acquaintance is impossible, thus unduly restricting the scope of genuine knowledge.

### 5. knowledge

**Definition:** A subject S knows how to do X if and only if (i) S possesses the relevant propositional knowledge that certain procedures constitute the correct way to do X, (ii) S can articulate or state these procedures, and (iii) S's ability to do X is explained by this propositional knowledge.

> It has sometimes been supposed that all knowledge is propositional in character, and that what we call knowing how to do something is, at bottom, knowing that certain propositions about the relevant procedures are true. On this intellectualist view, S knows how to do X if and only if (i) S possesses the relevant propositional knowledge that certain procedures constitute the correct way to do X, (ii) S can articulate or state these procedures, and (iii) S's ability to do X is explained by this propositional knowledge. The attractions of this reduction are plain enough, for it promises to unify the theory of knowledge under a single heading. Yet one might observe a case which reveals the insufficiency of this analysis. Consider a child of seven who has been riding a bicycle with great proficiency for several years. If asked how she maintains her balance, she is quite unable to say. She cannot articulate the principle that one must turn the handlebars in the direction of an incipient fall, nor does she know anything of the physics of angular momentum or gyroscopic precession. She simply gets on the bicycle and rides. It appears that her knowledge-how cannot be explained by any propositional knowledge she possesses, for she possesses none of the relevant propositions. The intellectualist must either deny that the child knows how to ride a bicycle, which seems absurd, or else attribute to her some unconscious propositional knowledge, which appears to be an ad hoc manoeuvre designed solely to preserve the theory. The more natural conclusion, as Ryle urged, is that knowledge-how is a distinct species of knowledge, consisting in a capacity or disposition to perform, and not reducible to the contemplation of truths.

*Missing condition:* The analysis fails to recognise that knowledge-how may consist in a practical capacity or disposition to act which is not reducible to, nor dependent upon, the possession of any corresponding propositional knowledge.

## AbdCx Examples

Each example presents background cases that motivate a definition, then constructs a scenario revealing what the background cases shared but the definition missed.

### 1. knowledge

**Definition:** A subject S knows that p if and only if (i) p is true, (ii) S believes that p, and (iii) S is justified in believing that p.

> We may begin by considering several cases in which it seems uncontroversial that a person possesses knowledge. A surveyor, employing calibrated instruments and established methods, measures the height of a cathedral spire and arrives at the figure of sixty-five metres; the spire is indeed sixty-five metres tall, and we should say without hesitation that the surveyor knows this. Again, a historian who consults multiple independent archives and thereby forms the belief that a certain treaty was signed in 1713 — the date being correct — is rightly said to know when the treaty was signed. Or consider the chemist who performs a litmus test, observes the paper turn red, and concludes that the solution is acidic, which it is. In each of these instances the subject holds a true belief and possesses what we should ordinarily regard as adequate justification for holding it.
>
> From such paradigm cases one might extract the following analysis: a subject S knows that p if and only if p is true, S believes that p, and S is justified in believing that p. This tripartite account has considerable intuitive appeal, and indeed it captures everything that is explicitly present in the cases just surveyed. Yet there is a case which reveals the insufficiency of this analysis. Suppose a man glances at a clock mounted on the facade of a public building and reads the time as two o'clock; he thereupon believes that it is two o'clock, and he is right — it is exactly two o'clock. However, the clock has been stopped for several days and shows two o'clock at all hours. It is purely by accident that the man happened to consult it at the one moment in the day when it displayed the correct time. His belief is true, and his justification — namely, reading an apparently functioning public timepiece — is of precisely the sort we accept in ordinary contexts. Nevertheless, we should be most reluctant to say that he knows what time it is. The background cases all exhibited a feature which the definition failed to record: in each of them, the connection between the justification and the truth of the belief was not merely coincidental. The surveyor's instruments were causally responsive to the actual height of the spire; the historian's archives bore a reliable evidential relation to the historical facts. The tripartite analysis, by requiring only that justified belief and truth obtain simultaneously, neglects to demand that the justification be appropriately connected to the fact believed, and it is this omission that permits cases of lucky coincidence to satisfy the conditions whilst plainly failing to constitute knowledge.

*Abductive insight:* The background cases each exhibited a non-accidental connection between the fact believed and the ground of belief, yet the definition captured only the presence of justification, not its actually producing the truth of the belief in the right way. What the abstraction lost was the requirement that it not be a matter of luck that the justified belief turned out true.

### 2. knowledge

**Definition:** A subject S knows that p if and only if (i) p is true, (ii) S believes that p, and (iii) S's belief that p was formed by a cognitive process that is reliable — that is, one which produces a high ratio of true beliefs to false beliefs.

> Let us take note of several unproblematic instances of knowledge. A birdwatcher, possessed of decades of experience and practised skill, identifies a bird in good light as a goldfinch, and a goldfinch it is; we say he knows it to be a goldfinch. A radiologist, trained in the interpretation of chest X-rays, reads a film and correctly identifies a fracture; she knows there is a fracture. A native speaker of French hears a sentence and immediately judges it to be grammatically well-formed, and the judgement is correct; one would not hesitate to ascribe knowledge to her. In each case the subject arrives at a true belief through a cognitive process — perceptual recognition, trained diagnostic inference, linguistic competence — that characteristically produces true beliefs in the relevant domain.
>
> One might accordingly propose that knowledge is true belief produced by a reliable cognitive process, where reliability is understood as the tendency of the process to yield a high ratio of true beliefs to false beliefs. This reliabilist account seems well suited to the cases above, for in each instance the process in question has an impressive track record of accuracy. However, there is a case which reveals the insufficiency of this analysis. Suppose a person has been raised on a distant planet where atmospheric refraction systematically distorts the apparent positions of objects, so that his visual faculties produce overwhelmingly false beliefs about spatial location in that environment — they are, by any reasonable standard, unreliable there. He is then transported, without his knowledge, to Earth, where conditions of observation are normal and his perceptual faculties happen to function with a high degree of accuracy. He looks at a barn and forms the belief that a barn stands directly before him, which is true, and his visual system is, in the terrestrial environment, a reliable producer of such beliefs. Yet there is something deeply unsatisfying about crediting him with knowledge. The difficulty is that the definition requires only that the process be reliable, without specifying the environment in which its reliability is to be assessed. In all of the background cases the process had been exercised and had demonstrated its reliability in the very environment in which the belief was formed. The abstraction from cases to definition lost this environmental anchoring, and the result is an analysis that can be satisfied by a process that is reliable only by the accident of the subject's having been placed in a congenial environment rather than by any stable epistemic virtue.

*Abductive insight:* The background cases all involved processes operating in the environment in which their reliability had been established. The definition abstracts away the dependency on environment, recording only that the process be reliable simpliciter, and thereby fails to specify whether reliability must hold in the environment where the belief is actually formed or in the environment in which the process was developed.

### 3. knowledge

**Definition:** A subject S knows that p if and only if (i) p is true, (ii) S believes that p, (iii) S is justified in believing that p, and (iv) there is no genuine defeater — that is, no true proposition which, were S to become aware of it, would undermine S's justification for believing that p.

> It is instructive to consider cases in which knowledge appears most secure. A juror believes the defendant guilty upon the strength of compelling eyewitness testimony, corroborating forensic evidence, and the defendant's own partial admission; there are no countervailing considerations, and the defendant is in fact guilty. A scientist believes that a chemical compound possesses a certain molecular structure, her belief resting upon multiple convergent spectroscopic analyses with no anomalous results, and the structure is indeed as she supposes. A navigator, having taken a careful celestial observation confirmed by an independent sighting, believes the ship to be at a certain latitude, and so it is. In each of these cases the subject holds a justified true belief, and there appears to be no true proposition lurking in the background which, were the subject to learn of it, would rationally require the abandonment of the belief.
>
> One might therefore propose that knowledge is justified true belief for which there exists no genuine defeater — no true proposition which, were the subject to become aware of it, would undermine the justification for the belief in question. This no-defeater condition appears to capture what distinguishes the foregoing cases from mere lucky true belief. Yet there is a case which reveals the insufficiency of this analysis. Suppose a doctor examines a patient and correctly diagnoses a rare condition on the basis of textbook symptoms. It happens that a true report, published in an obscure foreign journal, describes a superficially similar but distinct condition that presents with precisely the same symptoms in a small number of patients. Were the doctor to encounter this report, it would quite reasonably shake her confidence in the original diagnosis and might lead her to suspend judgement pending further tests. The no-defeater condition is thus violated: there exists a true proposition — the content of the report — which would undermine her justification. And yet her diagnosis is correct; the similar condition described in the report is not present in this patient; her evidence genuinely supports the conclusion she has drawn. We should, it seems, wish to say that she knows what ails her patient. The background cases all enjoyed the feature that any true proposition capable of undermining the subject's justification would also have indicated that the belief was in fact false. The definition, in demanding the absence of all true undermining propositions, failed to discriminate between defeaters that reveal a genuine deficiency in the subject's epistemic position and those that are merely misleading — true propositions that would confuse but would not track any actual error.

*Abductive insight:* The background cases shared the feature that any true proposition capable of undermining the subject's justification would also have pointed towards the belief's being false. The definition failed to distinguish between defeaters that genuinely indicate error and those that merely appear to — it demands the absence of all true undermining propositions, even those that are misleading.

### 4. knowledge

**Definition:** A subject S knows that p if and only if (i) p is true, (ii) S believes that p, and (iii) S's belief that p is caused by the fact that p.

> There are cases in which the causal ancestry of a belief seems central to its status as knowledge. A child who touches a hot stove and feels pain thereby acquires the belief that the stove is hot; the heat of the stove is the cause, through a natural and intelligible process, of the very belief whose content it constitutes. A person who sees a red post-box in broad daylight and believes there to be a red post-box before him arrives at his belief through a causal process initiated by the object of belief itself. A geologist who examines a rock formation and concludes that the strata were deposited during the Jurassic period traces a causal path from the geological facts through perception and trained inference to her belief. In each instance we are inclined to say that the subject knows, and in each instance there is a causal chain running from the fact believed to the belief about it.
>
> One might therefore propose that knowledge is true belief caused by the fact that makes the belief true. This causal theory has the virtue of explaining why knowledge seems to require more than the mere conjunction of truth and belief — it requires a real connection between the two. Yet there is a case which reveals the insufficiency of this analysis. Suppose a man comes to believe that a certain building is on fire. The building is on fire, and the fire is indeed the distal cause of his belief — but the causal route is deviant. The fire causes a short circuit in the building's wiring; the short circuit causes the fire alarm to malfunction and remain silent; the silence of the alarm causes a passing electrician to investigate the wiring fault; the electrician mentions the fault to the man, who by an irrational leap of imagination infers that the building must be ablaze. The causal condition of the definition is satisfied, for the fact that the building is on fire is a cause — indeed, the initiating cause — of the man's belief. Yet we should hardly wish to say that he knows the building to be on fire; his belief, though caused by the relevant fact, arrives at truth by a route that is epistemically fortuitous. The difficulty is that in all the background cases the causal chain from fact to belief ran through processes — normal perception, disciplined inference — that were constitutively apt for producing beliefs whose content reflects the facts. The definition recorded only that the fact cause the belief, without attending to whether the manner of causation was of the sort that renders belief epistemically responsive to reality.

*Abductive insight:* In the background cases, the causal chain from fact to belief ran through processes — perception, trained inference — that were such as to make the belief's content sensitive to the fact's obtaining. The definition required only that the fact cause the belief, without stipulating that the causal chain be of an epistemically appropriate kind.

### 5. knowledge

**Definition:** A subject S knows that p if and only if (i) p is true, (ii) S believes that p, and (iii) in nearby possible worlds in which p is false, S does not believe that p — that is, S could not easily have been wrong.

> Let us consider several cases in which a person's true belief appears to be, in some important sense, non-accidental. A mathematics student proves a theorem with care and rigour; her understanding of the material is thorough and her concentration exemplary, so that there is no nearby possibility in which she would have made an error in the proof. We say she knows the theorem. A man identifies his own bicycle in a rack containing twenty others; he has owned it for years and is familiar with every scratch and dent, so that there is no close possible situation in which he would mistake a different bicycle for his own. He knows which bicycle is his. A woman tastes her morning tea and believes it to contain milk; her palate is sufficiently refined that in no proximate situation would she form this belief falsely. She knows the tea contains milk.
>
> From these cases one might extract the condition that knowledge requires safety: in nearby possible worlds in which the proposition believed is false, the subject does not believe it. The knower, on this account, is one who could not easily have been wrong. This condition neatly captures what the foregoing cases share — a robustness of true belief across close possibilities. Yet there is a case which reveals the insufficiency of this analysis. Suppose a man stands before a barn — a perfectly real, ordinary barn — and believes that there is a barn before him. However, the surrounding countryside is, unbeknown to him, populated with elaborate barn facades: mere painted fronts, visually indistinguishable from genuine barns, erected for purposes of a film production. He happens to be looking at the sole real barn in the vicinity. In the nearest possible worlds in which there is no real barn before him — that is, worlds in which he stands before one of the facades — he would still believe himself to be looking at a barn. The safety condition is violated, for he could very easily have been wrong. And yet there is a strong intuitive pull towards saying that, as things actually stand, he is looking at a real barn and his perceptual faculties are functioning normally — surely he sees that it is a barn. The matter is, at the very least, far from clear. What the background cases shared, and what the definition failed to make explicit, was that the method of belief-formation reliably discriminated the target state of affairs from the relevant alternatives present in the actual environment. The safety condition, by attending only to what the subject would believe in counterfactual scenarios, neglected the question of whether the subject's method is appropriately sensitive to the local epistemic landscape.

*Abductive insight:* The background cases each involved a method of belief-formation that was discriminating across the range of relevant local alternatives. The definition required only that the subject would not believe p in nearby worlds where p is false, but it failed to capture that the method must be able to distinguish the actual case from the relevant alternatives present in the subject's environment.
