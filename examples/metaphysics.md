## DefCx Examples

Each example presents a definition as necessary and sufficient conditions, then constructs a scenario that reveals the definition's insufficiency.

### 1. substance

**Definition:** An entity is a substance if and only if (i) it is a particular, (ii) it is capable of independent existence, and (iii) it persists through change in its properties.

> It is tempting to define substance as follows: an entity is a substance if and only if it is a particular, it is capable of independent existence, and it persists through change in its properties. This definition captures the paradigmatic cases — a horse, a stone, a man — each of which is a particular thing, exists independently of other particulars, and endures through alteration of its accidental properties. Yet the definition admits cases that one should hesitate to call substances. Consider a wave upon the open ocean. It is a particular: one may point to it, track its progress, and distinguish it from neighbouring waves. It persists through changes in its properties, for as it moves across the sea its height increases and diminishes, its speed varies with the depth of the water, and its shape alters as it encounters currents and obstacles. And it appears to enjoy independent existence, travelling freely for hundreds of miles without dependence upon any other particular entity. All three conditions are satisfied. Yet a wave is not a substance. It is a disturbance propagated through a medium; the water through which it passes does not travel with it, and the wave possesses no matter of its own. The definition has failed to require that the entity possess its own constituent matter or intrinsic principle of unity, and so it admits what is merely a pattern of motion as though it were a genuine substance.

*Missing condition:* The definition fails to require that the entity possess its own constituent matter or intrinsic principle of unity; a wave satisfies all three conditions yet is merely a pattern of motion in a medium, not a substance in any philosophically serious sense.

### 2. substance

**Definition:** An entity is a substance if and only if (i) it is the bearer of properties, (ii) it is not itself a property of anything, and (iii) it is spatiotemporally located.

> One might propose that an entity is a substance if and only if it is the bearer of properties, it is not itself a property of anything, and it is spatiotemporally located. The definition is attractive in its economy: it distinguishes substances from their properties, from relations, and from abstract entities at a single stroke. A horse bears properties — colour, shape, mass — is not a property of anything else, and occupies a definite region of space at each moment of its existence. The definition captures such cases admirably. Yet it encounters a difficulty when one considers the objects of mathematics. Take the number seven. It is the bearer of properties: it is odd, it is prime, it is the sum of three and four. It is not itself a property of anything; the Platonist regards it as an object in its own right, not as an attribute predicated of collections. But it is not spatiotemporally located. The number seven does not exist in London or in the year 1927; it exists, if it exists at all, outside space and time altogether. The third condition fails. If one holds, as a great many philosophers from Plato onwards have held, that numbers are self-subsistent objects — substances in the fullest sense — then the definition is too narrow. It has mistaken a feature of concrete substances for an essential feature of substance as such.

*Missing condition:* The definition restricts substance to the spatiotemporal, but if abstract objects such as numbers are admitted as genuine entities — as Platonists maintain — then the requirement of spatiotemporal location excludes a class of objects that have as strong a claim to substantiality as any concrete particular.

### 3. causation metaphysical

**Definition:** An event C is the cause of an event E if and only if (i) C is a sufficient condition for E, (ii) C temporally precedes E, and (iii) there is no further event D such that D is sufficient for E and D is temporally between C and E.

> One might define causation as follows: an event C is the cause of an event E if and only if C is a sufficient condition for E, C temporally precedes E, and there is no further event D such that D is sufficient for E and temporally falls between C and E. The third clause is intended to screen off remote causes in favour of proximate ones. This definition works tolerably well for simple causal chains: the spark causes the explosion, the virus causes the illness, the frost causes the pipe to burst. Yet it founders upon cases of causal overdetermination. Suppose a man strikes a match in a room filled with oxygen, and at the very same instant a second man holds a lit taper to the match head. Either event alone would have been sufficient to light the match. The striking temporally precedes the lighting, and so does the application of the taper; neither is temporally between the other and the effect. The definition cannot say which event is the cause, for both satisfy all three conditions equally. Nor can it say that both are causes, for it is framed in terms of a single sufficient condition. Cases of this sort — where two independent causes each suffice for the effect — reveal that the definition has built into itself an assumption of causal uniqueness that the world does not always respect.

*Missing condition:* The definition assumes that causation is a relation between a single sufficient condition and its effect, but cases of overdetermination show that multiple independently sufficient conditions may jointly or severally produce the same effect, and the definition has no resources to handle such multiplicity.

### 4. time

**Definition:** A duration is a period of time if and only if (i) it is measurable by a clock, (ii) it is occupied by events or processes, and (iii) it is part of a single linear temporal order.

> One might propose that a duration is a period of time if and only if it is measurable by a clock, it is occupied by events or processes, and it is part of a single linear temporal order. This definition captures the ordinary understanding of temporal duration: the hour of a lecture, the day of labour, the year of study — each is measurable, each is filled with events, and each takes its place in the continuous succession of moments that constitutes the temporal order. Yet the definition encounters a difficulty with dreamless sleep. A man retires at eleven o'clock and wakes at seven; eight hours have elapsed. The clock has measured the interval, and the interval is part of the linear temporal order. But from the standpoint of the sleeper, the duration is not occupied by any events or processes. There are no experiences, no thoughts, no dreams — nothing whatever fills the interval. It is, from his perspective, as though the eight hours did not exist. If the second condition is construed subjectively, it fails. If it is construed objectively — the heart beats, the blood circulates, the earth rotates — then it is satisfied, but only by shifting the meaning of 'occupied' from the events of the subject's experience to events external to or beneath his awareness. The definition, it appears, equivocates on a crucial term.

*Missing condition:* The definition conflates the passage of time with the occurrence of experienced events; a period of time may elapse without any events of which a given subject is aware, and the definition must either relativise 'occupation' to a perspective or abandon the requirement altogether.

### 5. time

**Definition:** An instant is a moment of time if and only if (i) it has no temporal duration, (ii) it divides the temporal continuum into a past and a future, and (iii) some event occurs at it.

> One might define a moment of time as an instant that has no temporal duration, divides the temporal continuum into a past and a future, and is such that some event occurs at it. The first two conditions are structural: they characterise the moment as a durationless point that separates what has been from what is to come. The third condition ties the moment to the world of events — it requires that something happen at the instant in question. This seems reasonable enough, for we ordinarily identify moments by the events that occur at them: the moment of the explosion, the moment of the eclipse, the moment at which the train departed. Yet the definition proves too restrictive. Consider a moment at which nothing whatever happens. No event occurs, no state changes, no process begins or ends. The universe is, at that instant, entirely quiescent. Such a moment has no temporal duration and divides the continuum into past and future; the first two conditions are satisfied. But the third condition fails, for no event occurs at the instant. The definition would deny that it is a moment of time at all. Yet this is plainly wrong. The instant exists; it occupies its position in the temporal order between two neighbouring instants. Time does not cease to flow merely because nothing happens. The definition has confused the existence of an instant with the occurrence of an event at that instant.

*Missing condition:* The definition makes the existence of a temporal instant contingent upon the occurrence of an event at that instant, but time may pass without events, and the instant exists in virtue of its position in the temporal order, not in virtue of its being occupied.

## AbdCx Examples

Each example presents background cases that motivate a definition, then constructs a scenario revealing what the background cases shared but the definition missed.

### 1. substance

**Definition:** An entity is a substance if and only if (i) it is a self-subsistent particular, (ii) it is composed of matter, and (iii) it has a principle of unity that makes it one thing rather than many.

> Consider three cases of substance. A horse is a substance: it is a unified, self-subsistent particular that persists through change, possessing its own matter organised by a vital principle. A stone is a substance: it is an individual object with a determinate identity and a definite material composition. A man is a substance: he is a particular enduring thing, composed of flesh and bone, capable of independent existence. From these cases one might extract a definition: an entity is a substance if and only if it is a self-subsistent particular, it is composed of matter, and it has a principle of unity that makes it one thing rather than many.
>
> But this definition, drawn from material particulars, proves too restrictive. Consider the soul as conceived by the Cartesian tradition. The soul is a self-subsistent particular — it exists independently of the body, surviving, on the Cartesian account, the destruction of the material frame. It has a principle of unity, namely consciousness: it is one thinking thing, not a collection of disparate processes. But it is not composed of matter; it is an immaterial substance, a res cogitans, wholly distinct from the extended material world. The second condition fails. Yet the Cartesian tradition insists, with considerable philosophical force, that the soul is a substance in the fullest sense — indeed, the substance of which we have the most immediate and certain knowledge. The background cases were all material things, and the definition mistook their shared materiality for a necessary feature of substance. The concept of substance, however, was always intended to reach beyond the material.

*Abductive insight:* The background cases were all material particulars — a horse, a stone, a man — and the definition abstracted the material composition they share. But the concept of substance was historically intended to cover immaterial as well as material entities, and the background cases were too uniform in their materiality to reveal this.

### 2. causation metaphysical

**Definition:** An event C causes an event E if and only if (i) C is connected to E by a continuous physical mechanism, (ii) C temporally precedes E, and (iii) the mechanism transmits a conserved physical quantity from C to E.

> Consider three cases of causation. Striking a match causes it to ignite: the striking precedes the ignition, a continuous physical mechanism connects them, and kinetic energy is transmitted from the striker to the match head. A blow from a hammer causes a nail to sink: the blow precedes the sinking, the mechanism of energy transfer is straightforward, and a conserved quantity — momentum — passes from hammer to nail. Heating water causes it to boil: the heat precedes the boiling, the mechanism runs through the agitation of water molecules, and thermal energy is transmitted continuously. From these cases one might define causation: an event C causes an event E if and only if C is connected to E by a continuous physical mechanism, C temporally precedes E, and the mechanism transmits a conserved physical quantity from C to E.
>
> But this definition, drawn from cases of positive physical interaction, cannot accommodate causation by omission. Consider a gardener who neglects to water his plants during a drought. The plants die. The gardener's failure to water is, by any ordinary reckoning, the cause of their death. Yet the failure is not an event in any positive sense — it is an absence, a non-occurrence. There is no continuous physical mechanism connecting the absence of watering to the death of the plants; an absence does not transmit anything. No conserved physical quantity passes from the gardener's inaction to the wilting leaves. The first and third conditions fail entirely. The background cases all involved positive events in which physical quantities were transmitted through material mechanisms, and the definition abstracted these features as essential to causation. But causation by omission — which is ubiquitous in both everyday life and the law — involves no mechanism, no transmission, and no positive event. The definition has generalised from too narrow a base.

*Abductive insight:* The background cases all involved positive events transmitting physical quantities through continuous mechanisms. The definition abstracted these features as essential. But causation by omission — the failure to act — involves no mechanism, no transmission, and no positive event, yet it is as genuine a form of causation as any.

### 3. time

**Definition:** The temporal interval between two events is determinate if and only if (i) both events can be assigned positions on a single time axis, (ii) the interval between them is the same for all observers, and (iii) the interval can be measured by any sufficiently accurate clock.

> Consider three cases of temporal measurement. The duration of a footrace is measured by a single clock: the starter fires the pistol and the runner crosses the finish line, and the elapsed time is determinate and unambiguous. The duration of a day is measured by the rotation of the earth: midnight to midnight, twenty-four hours, the same for all who stand upon the surface. The duration of a pendulum's swing is measured by counting oscillations against a standard clock: the interval is precise and reproducible. From these cases one might propose: the temporal interval between two events is determinate if and only if both events can be assigned positions on a single time axis, the interval between them is the same for all observers, and the interval can be measured by any sufficiently accurate clock.
>
> But this definition, drawn from cases in which all events and observers are at rest relative to one another, proves untenable when relative motion enters the picture. Consider two events: a flash of light on earth and a flash of light on a spaceship travelling at a significant fraction of the speed of light. Both events can be assigned positions on a time axis, and both can be measured by clocks. But the temporal interval between them is not the same for all observers. The observer on earth and the observer on the spaceship will measure different elapsed times — not because their clocks are inaccurate, but because time itself passes at different rates for observers in relative motion. This is the relativistic dilation of time, confirmed by experiment to extraordinary precision. The second condition fails. The background cases all involved a world of rest, in which temporal intervals are indeed the same for everyone. The definition mistook this feature of the stationary world for a necessary feature of time as such.

*Abductive insight:* The background cases all involved events at rest relative to one another and to the measuring clock. The definition abstracted the observer-independence of temporal intervals as essential. But relativistic physics reveals that temporal intervals are observer-dependent when the events or observers are in relative motion, and this dependence is not an imperfection of measurement but a feature of the nature of time itself.

### 4. identity metaphysical

**Definition:** An object that has undergone the replacement of a part is the same object if and only if (i) the majority of its parts remain original, (ii) the replacement was gradual rather than sudden, and (iii) the function of the object was preserved throughout.

> Consider three cases of replacement. A ship has one plank replaced: it remains the same ship. A cathedral has its roof repaired: it remains the same cathedral. A man loses a tooth and receives a prosthetic: he remains the same man. From these cases one might define identity through replacement: an object that has undergone the replacement of a part is the same object if and only if the majority of its parts remain original, the replacement was gradual rather than sudden, and the function of the object was preserved throughout.
>
> But this definition, drawn from cases of partial replacement, founders upon total replacement. Consider the classical puzzle of the ship of Theseus. Over a period of years, every plank of the ship is removed and replaced by a new plank, one at a time. At each stage the replacement is gradual — only one plank is changed — and the function of the ship is preserved; it remains seaworthy and in service throughout. But eventually every original plank has been replaced; the majority of parts are not original — indeed, none of the parts are original. The first condition fails. Yet it seems natural to say that the ship is the same ship. At no moment in the process did it cease to be the ship of Theseus; at no moment was its identity disrupted. The background cases all involved partial replacement, and the definition mistook the partiality — the fact that most parts remained — for a necessary condition of identity-preservation. But the ship of Theseus shows that identity can survive total replacement, provided the replacement is sufficiently gradual.

*Abductive insight:* The background cases all involved partial replacement — one plank, one roof, one tooth — and the definition abstracted the majority-original requirement from their shared feature. But the ship of Theseus shows that gradual total replacement preserves identity just as effectively as partial replacement, provided the process is sufficiently incremental. The definition mistook the partiality of the background replacements for a necessary condition of identity-preservation.

### 5. universals

**Definition:** A property is a genuine universal if and only if (i) it is instantiated by multiple particulars, (ii) each instance possesses the property in the same way, and (iii) the property admits of no degrees.

> Consider three cases of universals. Redness is a universal: it is instantiated by many particular red things — an apple, a rose, a sunset — and each instance shares the same quality. Triangularity is a universal: it is instantiated by many particular triangles, and each exhibits the same geometrical form. Hardness is a universal: it is instantiated by many particular hard things, and each possesses the same resistance to deformation. From these cases one might define a genuine universal as a property that is instantiated by multiple particulars, is possessed by each instance in the same way, and admits of no degrees.
>
> But this definition, drawn from apparently uniform properties, proves too restrictive. Consider temperature. It is instantiated by many particulars: this cup of tea has a temperature, that block of ice has a temperature, the surface of the sun has a temperature. Temperature functions as a universal in every logical respect — it is predicable of many things, it supports generalisations, it enters into laws of nature. But it admits of degrees: the tea is at seventy degrees, the ice at minus ten, the sun at five thousand. The third condition fails. Yet temperature is as genuine a property as redness or triangularity. Indeed, upon reflection, even the background cases admit of degrees: redness ranges from scarlet to crimson, triangularity encompasses acute, right, and obtuse triangles, and hardness is measured on a continuous scale. The definition imposed a requirement of absolute uniformity that even its own paradigm cases do not strictly satisfy. What makes a property a universal is its predicability of many, not its freedom from variation.

*Abductive insight:* The background cases — redness, triangularity, hardness — were chosen so that each instance resembles every other in a seemingly uniform way. But redness admits of shades, triangularity admits of shapes, and hardness admits of degrees on the Mohs scale. The definition imposed a uniformity that even the background cases do not strictly satisfy, and properties that vary continuously — temperature, mass, brightness — are universals despite their variation.
