## DefCx Examples

Each example presents a definition as necessary and sufficient conditions, then constructs a scenario that reveals the definition's insufficiency.

### 1. community

**Definition:** A group of persons constitutes a community if and only if (i) they inhabit a common geographical area, (ii) they interact with one another on a regular basis, and (iii) they share a set of customs or practices.

> One might propose that a group of persons constitutes a community if and only if they inhabit a common geographical area, they interact with one another on a regular basis, and they share a set of customs or practices. This definition captures the village, the town, the urban neighbourhood: in each case, persons live near one another, meet in the course of daily life, and observe common practices. Yet the definition proves too narrow when one considers dispersed communities. The Jewish diaspora of mediaeval Europe is a case in point. Its members were scattered across the kingdoms and principalities of the continent, separated by hundreds of miles, different languages, and often hostile borders. A Jew in Toledo might never meet a Jew in Cracow. Regular interaction in any direct sense was impossible for the great majority. Yet they shared religious customs, a liturgical language, a body of sacred texts, and a profound sense of collective belonging. No historian would deny that they constituted a community. The first and second conditions fail, yet the community persists. The definition has mistaken geographical proximity and face-to-face interaction — features of the local community — for essential features of community as such.

*Missing condition:* The definition requires geographical co-presence and regular interaction, but a community may be sustained by shared identity, tradition, and symbolic bonds across great distances without physical proximity.

### 2. community

**Definition:** A group of persons constitutes a community if and only if (i) membership is voluntary, (ii) the members recognise one another as fellow members, and (iii) the group pursues a common good.

> It is sometimes held that a group of persons constitutes a community if and only if membership is voluntary, the members recognise one another as fellow members, and the group pursues a common good. This account captures certain communities well: a guild of craftsmen, a religious brotherhood, a philosophical society. In each case the members have chosen to join, they know one another as fellows, and they work towards a shared end. Yet the definition encounters a difficulty with communities into which persons are born. Consider a small rural village in which generations of families have lived side by side. A child born in that village is a member of the community from the moment of his birth. He is recognised by the other villagers, he participates in the festivals and labours of the place, and the village pursues its common good — the maintenance of the church, the upkeep of the roads, the management of the common land. But his membership was not voluntary; he did not choose to belong. He was born there, as his parents were before him. The first condition fails. The definition has taken a feature of elective associations — voluntary entry — and imposed it upon community as such, when in truth many of the deepest communities are those one is born into rather than those one chooses to join.

*Missing condition:* The definition demands voluntary entry, but many of the most significant communities — the village, the nation, the linguistic group — are joined by birth rather than by choice.

### 3. tradition

**Definition:** A practice constitutes a tradition if and only if (i) it has been transmitted from one generation to the next over a considerable period, (ii) the practitioners are conscious of its antiquity, and (iii) it remains substantially unchanged across generations.

> A natural account holds that a practice constitutes a tradition if and only if it has been transmitted from one generation to the next over a considerable period, the practitioners are conscious of its antiquity, and it remains substantially unchanged across generations. This definition captures the paradigm cases: the celebration of harvest festivals in English villages, the recitation of ancestral genealogies in certain cultures, the observance of ancient religious rites. Each has been handed down over centuries, each is felt to be ancient, and each retains a recognisable form. Yet consider the Christmas tree. The practice of decorating an evergreen tree at Christmas became widespread in England only after the eighteen-forties, when the Royal household popularised the custom. Within scarcely a generation, the Christmas tree was embraced across the country and spoken of as a cherished tradition. But in 1860, the practice had not been transmitted over a considerable period — it was barely twenty years old in most households. And the practitioners could not have been conscious of great antiquity, for the custom was palpably recent. The first and second conditions fail. Yet the Christmas tree was already experienced and described as a tradition. The definition has insisted upon temporal depth, when a practice may acquire the character of a tradition through its ritual significance and its association with communal life, even when its actual history is brief.

*Missing condition:* The definition requires long transmission and consciousness of antiquity, but traditions may be rapidly adopted and experienced as traditional by virtue of their ritual character, their association with important occasions, and their felt connection to communal identity.

### 4. tradition

**Definition:** A practice constitutes a tradition if and only if (i) it is performed in the same manner each time it occurs, (ii) it serves a social function recognised by the community, and (iii) deviation from the practice is met with disapproval.

> One might define a tradition as a practice that is performed in the same manner each time it occurs, serves a social function recognised by the community, and is such that deviation from it is met with disapproval. This definition captures certain traditions well: the fixed liturgy of a church service, the prescribed steps of a ceremonial dance, the unvarying words of a toast at a formal dinner. In each case the practice is repeated identically, it serves a recognised purpose, and departures from the established form are censured. Yet consider the tradition of jazz improvisation as practised in New Orleans. The essence of the tradition is that each performance must differ from every other. The musicians take a familiar melody and transform it through spontaneous invention; to play the same solo twice would be to violate the very spirit of the art. The tradition serves a recognised social function — it is central to the musical culture of the city and to the identity of its practitioners. And deviation from the expectation of improvisation would indeed be met with disapproval. But the practice is emphatically not performed in the same manner each time. The first condition fails. The definition has mistaken the rigidity of certain traditions for an essential feature of tradition as such, overlooking those traditions whose vitality lies in creative variation rather than in faithful repetition.

*Missing condition:* The definition equates tradition with invariant repetition, but some traditions consist precisely in the expectation of creative variation within a recognised framework.

### 5. custom

**Definition:** A pattern of behaviour constitutes a custom if and only if (i) it is practised by the majority of a given society, (ii) it is transmitted by imitation rather than by formal instruction, and (iii) it carries no legal sanction for non-observance.

> A plausible account holds that a pattern of behaviour constitutes a custom if and only if it is practised by the majority of a given society, it is transmitted by imitation rather than by formal instruction, and it carries no legal sanction for non-observance. This definition captures familiar customs: shaking hands upon meeting, removing one's hat indoors, sending cards at Christmas. Each is practised by most people, learned informally, and not enforced by law. Yet consider the custom of wearing mourning dress after a bereavement. In British society, this custom was long observed with great solemnity: the bereaved wore black for a prescribed period, the depth and duration of mourning varying according to the closeness of the relationship. The custom was transmitted by imitation and social expectation, and there was no legal penalty for disregarding it. But at any given moment, only a small minority of the population — those who had recently suffered a loss — were actually practising the custom. The majority were not in mourning. The first condition fails. Yet no one would deny that wearing mourning dress was a custom of English society. The definition has confused the universality of a custom's recognition with the universality of its active practice, when in truth a custom may apply only in particular circumstances and be practised only by those to whom those circumstances pertain.

*Missing condition:* The definition requires majority practice, but a custom need only be recognised and observed by those to whom it applies in the relevant circumstances; it need not be actively practised by most members of the society at any given moment.

## AbdCx Examples

Each example presents background cases that motivate a definition, then constructs a scenario revealing what the background cases shared but the definition missed.

### 1. community

**Definition:** A group of persons constitutes a community if and only if (i) they reside in a compact, bounded locality, (ii) they depend upon one another for economic survival, and (iii) they participate in shared rituals at regular intervals.

> Consider three cases of community. A fishing village on the Devon coast, where families have lived together for generations, cooperating in the upkeep of the harbour and the observance of local festivals. A Bavarian market town whose inhabitants gather in the square, attend the same church, and celebrate the same traditions. A farming hamlet in Oxfordshire where families share the harvest, maintain the common green, and resolve disputes through the parish council. From these cases one might extract a definition: a group of persons constitutes a community if and only if they reside in a compact, bounded locality, they depend upon one another for economic survival, and they participate in shared rituals at regular intervals.
>
> But this definition, drawn from rural, place-bound communities, proves too narrow when applied to other forms of common life. Consider the community of classical scholars in nineteenth-century Europe. Its members — philologists, historians, editors of ancient texts — were scattered across the universities of the continent, from Edinburgh to Heidelberg to Padua. They did not reside in a single locality; they lived hundreds of miles apart. They did not depend upon one another for economic survival; each was employed and paid by his own institution. And they participated in no shared rituals beyond the occasional learned congress. Yet they constituted a community in a deep and important sense. They read one another's works with care, corresponded across national boundaries, debated one another's interpretations, and shared a profound sense of belonging to a common intellectual enterprise — the recovery and understanding of the ancient world. The background cases were all communities bound by place, livelihood, and ritual. The definition mistook these particular features for universal requirements, overlooking the communities that are sustained by shared purpose, mutual recognition, and the invisible bonds of intellectual fellowship.

*Abductive insight:* The background cases were all rural, place-bound communities in which proximity, economic interdependence, and ritual were naturally intertwined. The definition abstracted these features as essential, but the scholarly community shows that a community may be constituted by shared intellectual purpose, mutual recognition, and sustained communication, without proximity, economic dependence, or common ritual.

### 2. tradition

**Definition:** A practice constitutes a tradition if and only if (i) it has been faithfully preserved in its original form, (ii) its practitioners regard deviation as impermissible, and (iii) its continuity depends upon an authoritative custodian — a priest, a master, a guild — who ensures its correct transmission.

> Consider three cases of tradition. The recitation of the Homeric poems at the Panathenaic festival in ancient Athens, preserved in substance over centuries. The coronation of the English monarch, whose essential rites have endured since the mediaeval period. The Passover seder, whose ritual and text have been transmitted with extraordinary fidelity across millennia. From these cases one might define tradition: a practice constitutes a tradition if and only if it has been faithfully preserved in its original form, its practitioners regard deviation as impermissible, and its continuity depends upon an authoritative custodian who ensures correct transmission.
>
> But this definition, drawn from ritual and ceremonial traditions, proves too restrictive when applied to intellectual and institutional traditions. Consider the English common law. It has been practised continuously for some eight centuries, and no one would deny that it is a tradition — perhaps the central tradition of English legal civilisation. Yet the common law has not been preserved in its original form; it has evolved profoundly, absorbing new principles, adapting old ones to changed circumstances, and sometimes overruling what was once regarded as settled. Deviation is not impermissible; on the contrary, the tradition expects each generation of judges to develop the law in response to new cases and new social conditions. And there is no single authoritative custodian: the tradition is maintained by the collective practice of judges, barristers, and legal scholars, none of whom possesses exclusive authority over its content. All three conditions fail. The background cases were ritual traditions sustained by fixity and guardianship. The definition mistook these features for the essence of tradition, when some of the most important traditions — the common law, the philosophical tradition, the scientific tradition — are characterised by continuous evolution rather than by faithful preservation.

*Abductive insight:* The background cases were all ritual traditions characterised by ceremonial fixity and priestly guardianship. The definition abstracted preservation, impermissibility of deviation, and authoritative custodianship as essential, but the common-law tradition shows that a tradition may consist in a continuously evolving body of practice, sustained not by fixity but by a shared method of development and adaptation.

### 3. institution

**Definition:** A social arrangement constitutes an institution if and only if (i) it was founded by a deliberate act of authority, (ii) it is governed by written statutes or a constitution, and (iii) it has a continuous history traceable to a particular date of foundation.

> Consider three institutions. The Bank of England, established by Act of Parliament in 1694, with a charter, a governor, and a defined remit. The University of Oxford, founded by papal authority, governed by its statutes, and administered by its officers. The Royal Society, incorporated by royal charter in 1662. From these cases one might define an institution: a social arrangement constitutes an institution if and only if it was founded by a deliberate act of authority, it is governed by written statutes or a constitution, and it has a continuous history traceable to a particular date of foundation.
>
> But this definition, drawn from chartered bodies, proves too narrow. Consider the institution of the market. In every civilisation, people have gathered to buy and sell goods: the agora of Athens, the forum of Rome, the mediaeval English market town. The market is a social institution of the first importance — it organises the exchange of goods, regulates prices through competition, and sustains the livelihood of entire communities. Yet the market was not founded by any deliberate act of authority. It arose spontaneously wherever human beings engaged in trade. It is not governed by a written constitution; its rules — the hours of trade, the standards of fair dealing, the conventions of bargaining — are customary rather than statutory. And its history cannot be traced to a particular date; markets are as old as settled human life, and no one can say when or where the first market was held. All three conditions fail. The background cases were chartered bodies created by acts of sovereign or ecclesiastical power. The definition mistook these features for the essence of all institutions, overlooking the spontaneous institutions that arise from repeated human interaction and are sustained by custom and convention rather than by deliberate design.

*Abductive insight:* The background cases were all chartered bodies created by sovereign or ecclesiastical authority. The definition treated deliberate foundation, written governance, and a traceable origin as essential, but the market reveals that institutions may emerge spontaneously from repeated human interaction, governed by custom rather than statute, and lacking any identifiable moment of creation.

### 4. cooperation

**Definition:** An interaction constitutes cooperation if and only if (i) the agents enter the arrangement with the expectation of reciprocal benefit, (ii) the terms of the cooperation are known to all parties in advance, and (iii) each agent's contribution is roughly proportional to the benefit he receives.

> Consider three cases of cooperation. Two farmers agree to help each other with the harvest, each assisting the other on alternate days. A group of villagers pool their resources to repair the village bridge, each contributing labour and materials. A pair of merchants share the cost of hiring a ship. From these cases one might extract a definition: an interaction constitutes cooperation if and only if the agents enter the arrangement with the expectation of reciprocal benefit, the terms are known to all in advance, and each agent's contribution is roughly proportional to the benefit he receives.
>
> But this definition, drawn from calculated human partnerships, proves too restrictive. Consider the cooperation of bees in a hive. Thousands of worker bees forage, build comb, feed larvae, and defend the colony. Their labour is coordinated, their roles are differentiated, and the result — the survival and reproduction of the colony — depends upon their joint activity. Yet the worker bees, which are sterile, receive no reproductive benefit; their contributions are not proportional to any personal gain. The terms of cooperation are not known in advance in any cognitive sense; the bees do not deliberate or negotiate. And the cooperation is not entered with the expectation of reciprocal benefit; the workers sacrifice their own reproductive potential for the colony. All three conditions fail. The background cases were human arrangements among self-interested agents who negotiated and calculated. The definition mistook these features for essential properties of cooperation, when the biological world provides examples of cooperation that arise from instinct and natural selection, without any cognitive awareness of terms, any expectation of personal return, or any proportionality between contribution and benefit.

*Abductive insight:* The background cases were all instances of deliberate human cooperation motivated by calculated self-interest. The definition abstracted expectation, foreknowledge, and proportionality as essential, but biological cooperation demonstrates that cooperative systems may operate through instinct and genetic programming, without any cognitive awareness of terms or expectation of personal return.

### 5. trust

**Definition:** A person trusts another if and only if (i) the trust is grounded in repeated positive interactions, (ii) the trusted person has demonstrated competence in the relevant domain, and (iii) the trusting person can identify specific reasons for her trust.

> Consider three cases of trust. A man trusts his solicitor, upon whom he has relied for years. A woman trusts her physician, who has treated her for two decades. A merchant trusts his business partner, with whom he has traded profitably for a decade. From these cases one might define trust: a person trusts another if and only if the trust is grounded in repeated positive interactions, the trusted person has demonstrated competence in the relevant domain, and the trusting person can identify specific reasons for her trust.
>
> But this definition, drawn from long-standing personal relationships, is too narrow. Consider a person who makes a charitable donation to a relief organisation operating in a country she has never visited. She trusts the organisation to use her money wisely — to deliver food, to build shelter, to provide medical care. Yet she has had no personal interaction with the organisation; she has never met its officers or observed its operations. She cannot assess its competence first-hand; she has only the testimony of others and the organisation's published accounts. And if asked to identify specific reasons for her trust, she may be unable to articulate more than a vague sense that the organisation is reputable and that charitable organisations in general are trustworthy. All three conditions fail. Yet her trust is genuine — she has parted with real money on the strength of it. The background cases were all bilateral relationships sustained by personal experience. The definition mistook this personal, experiential basis for an essential feature of trust, overlooking the impersonal trust that people place in institutions, in strangers, and in organisations they have never encountered, on the basis of reputation, social endorsement, and a generalised disposition to trust that is prior to any particular experience.

*Abductive insight:* The background cases all involved bilateral relationships built up through personal experience over time. The definition treated personal interaction, demonstrated competence, and articulable reasons as essential, but trust in institutions and strangers often rests upon reputation, social endorsement, and a generalised disposition to trust that precedes and is independent of personal experience.
