## DefCx Examples

Each example presents a definition as necessary and sufficient conditions, then constructs a scenario that reveals the definition's insufficiency.

### 1. explanation

**Definition:** An account of a phenomenon constitutes a scientific explanation if and only if (i) it cites a general law under which the phenomenon can be subsumed, (ii) it specifies the initial conditions from which, together with the law, the phenomenon can be deduced, and (iii) the deduction is logically valid.

> One might propose that an account of a phenomenon constitutes a scientific explanation if and only if it cites a general law under which the phenomenon can be subsumed, it specifies the initial conditions from which, together with the law, the phenomenon can be deduced, and the deduction is logically valid. This definition captures a good many explanations in physics and chemistry. When one explains the boiling of water at a certain temperature by citing the relevant gas laws and the atmospheric pressure, one has subsumed the phenomenon under a general law, specified the initial conditions, and produced a valid deduction. Yet consider the evolutionary explanation of the peacock's tail. Darwin accounted for this extravagant appendage by appealing to sexual selection: peahens prefer males with larger, more colourful tails, and this preference, operating over many generations, has produced the elaborate plumage we observe. This explanation is universally regarded as one of the great achievements of biological science. But it does not cite a law from which the peacock's tail can be deduced. The principles of natural and sexual selection do not entail that any particular species will develop any particular trait; they describe a mechanism that makes the trait intelligible without rendering it logically necessary. One cannot write down premises and derive the peacock's tail as a conclusion. The first and third conditions fail. The definition has taken the deductive structure of certain physical explanations and elevated it to a requirement for all scientific explanation, when in truth some of the most powerful explanations in science proceed by describing causal mechanisms rather than by subsuming phenomena under exceptionless laws.

*Missing condition:* The definition requires deduction from general laws, but many scientific explanations operate by describing mechanisms or selective processes that render phenomena intelligible without logically entailing them.

### 2. scientific law

**Definition:** A generalisation constitutes a scientific law if and only if (i) it is universally quantified, (ii) it admits of no exceptions, and (iii) it is supported by empirical evidence gathered through controlled experimentation.

> It is sometimes held that a generalisation constitutes a scientific law if and only if it is universally quantified, it admits of no exceptions, and it is supported by empirical evidence gathered through controlled experimentation. This account captures the paradigmatic laws of laboratory science: Boyle's law relating pressure and volume was established through careful experimental manipulation, and Ohm's law relating voltage, current, and resistance was similarly grounded in controlled measurement. Yet consider the laws of planetary motion set forth by Kepler in the early seventeenth century. These laws — that the planets move in ellipses with the sun at one focus, that the radius vector sweeps out equal areas in equal times, and that the squares of the periods are proportional to the cubes of the semi-major axes — are universally quantified and admit of no exceptions within their domain. They are among the most celebrated generalisations in the history of science. But they were not established through controlled experimentation. Kepler did not manipulate the orbits of the planets; he could not vary the conditions under which they moved. He derived his laws from the observational records compiled by Tycho Brahe, who had spent decades measuring the positions of the planets with unprecedented accuracy. These were data gathered by passive observation of the heavens, not by experimental intervention. The third condition fails. The definition has mistaken the method of the laboratory for a necessary condition of scientific lawhood, when some of the greatest laws of science have been established through the systematic analysis of naturally occurring phenomena that no human agent could control.

*Missing condition:* The definition requires controlled experimentation, but many scientific laws — particularly in astronomy and geology — are established through systematic observation of naturally occurring phenomena rather than through experimental manipulation.

### 3. theory

**Definition:** A body of propositions constitutes a scientific theory if and only if (i) it provides a unified explanation of a range of phenomena, (ii) it is formulated in precise mathematical language, and (iii) it is the product of a single coherent intellectual programme.

> One might hold that a body of propositions constitutes a scientific theory if and only if it provides a unified explanation of a range of phenomena, it is formulated in precise mathematical language, and it is the product of a single coherent intellectual programme. This definition captures certain theories admirably: Newton's mechanics, Maxwell's electrodynamics, and Einstein's general relativity are each formulated in mathematical language, each explains a range of phenomena in a unified manner, and each was developed as a coherent programme. Yet consider Darwin's theory of evolution by natural selection. It provides a unified explanation of an astonishing range of phenomena — the diversity of species, the adaptation of organisms to their environments, the geographical distribution of plants and animals, the existence of vestigial structures, the patterns revealed by comparative anatomy and embryology. No one would deny that it is a scientific theory of the first importance. But it was not formulated in precise mathematical language. Darwin's arguments in the Origin of Species are qualitative and discursive; they proceed by analogy, by the accumulation of examples, and by careful reasoning about causes and effects. There are no equations, no formal derivations, no mathematical models. The second condition fails. The definition has taken the mathematical character of the physical sciences and imposed it as a requirement upon all scientific theories, when in truth some of the most profound theories in the history of science have achieved their explanatory power through qualitative insight and causal reasoning rather than through mathematical formalism.

*Missing condition:* The definition demands mathematical formulation, but some of the most powerful scientific theories — particularly in biology and the earth sciences — achieve their explanatory force through qualitative concepts and causal reasoning rather than through mathematical formalisms.

### 4. observation

**Definition:** A report constitutes a scientific observation if and only if (i) it describes a state of affairs directly apprehended by the senses, (ii) the observer is in a position to perceive the phenomenon without the mediation of instruments, and (iii) any competent observer placed in the same circumstances would report the same thing.

> A natural account holds that a report constitutes a scientific observation if and only if it describes a state of affairs directly apprehended by the senses, the observer is in a position to perceive the phenomenon without the mediation of instruments, and any competent observer placed in the same circumstances would report the same thing. This definition captures the observations of early natural history well enough: a botanist who describes the colour and shape of a flower, a zoologist who records the behaviour of a bird, an astronomer who notes the position of a star with the naked eye. In each case the observer apprehends the phenomenon directly, without instrumental mediation, and any competent observer could confirm the report. Yet consider the observation of a distant galaxy through a radio telescope. The astronomer does not see the galaxy; the radio waves it emits lie far outside the visible spectrum. What she perceives is a pattern of signals displayed on a screen, signals that have been collected by a large antenna, amplified by electronic circuits, and processed by sophisticated software before they appear in any form intelligible to human perception. No unaided sense organ can detect radio waves at all. The first and second conditions fail. Yet the observation is accepted without hesitation by the scientific community; entire branches of astrophysics rest upon such instrument-mediated observations. The definition has identified observation with naked-eye perception, when the great expansion of scientific observation in the modern era has been achieved precisely by extending perception through instruments that detect what the unaided senses cannot.

*Missing condition:* The definition restricts observation to unaided sense perception, but much of modern science depends upon instrument-mediated observation, in which the observer perceives the output of instruments that detect phenomena lying beyond the range of the unaided senses.

### 5. reduction

**Definition:** A scientific theory T1 has been reduced to a theory T2 if and only if (i) every law of T1 can be derived as a logical consequence of the laws of T2 together with appropriate bridge principles, (ii) every term of T1 can be defined in terms of the vocabulary of T2, and (iii) T2 explains everything that T1 explains and more besides.

> It is sometimes proposed that a scientific theory T1 has been reduced to a theory T2 if and only if every law of T1 can be derived as a logical consequence of the laws of T2 together with appropriate bridge principles, every term of T1 can be defined in terms of the vocabulary of T2, and T2 explains everything that T1 explains and more besides. This definition has the virtue of clarity: reduction is a matter of logical derivation and definitional translation. It appears to capture the spirit of reductionism — the idea that one science is nothing over and above another, more fundamental science. Yet consider the most celebrated case of reduction in the history of science: the reduction of classical thermodynamics to statistical mechanics. One might suppose that the laws of thermodynamics are simply derivable from the mechanics of molecules. But the situation is more complicated than the definition allows. The second law of thermodynamics states that the entropy of an isolated system never decreases. In statistical mechanics, however, this corresponds not to a strict law but to an overwhelmingly probable tendency; there is always a minute but non-zero probability that entropy will spontaneously decrease. The thermodynamic law is not derivable as a logical consequence of statistical mechanics without additional idealising assumptions. Similarly, the concept of temperature in thermodynamics does not translate precisely into a single statistical-mechanical quantity without approximations that hold only in certain limiting cases. The first and second conditions fail in their strict form. Yet no philosopher of science would deny that statistical mechanics has provided a reductive account of thermodynamics. The definition has demanded a logical precision that actual scientific reductions do not exhibit, overlooking the approximate and idealised character of the correspondences that constitute real cases of reduction.

*Missing condition:* The definition requires exact derivability and precise translation of terms, but actual reductions in science are typically approximate, involving idealisations, limiting conditions, and correspondences that fall short of strict logical derivation.

## AbdCx Examples

Each example presents background cases that motivate a definition, then constructs a scenario revealing what the background cases shared but the definition missed.

### 1. explanation

**Definition:** An account of a phenomenon constitutes a scientific explanation if and only if (i) it deduces the phenomenon from a general law and a statement of initial conditions, (ii) the law is a well-established empirical generalisation, and (iii) the explanation renders the phenomenon nomically expectable.

> Consider three cases of scientific explanation. The fall of a stone is explained by citing Newton's gravitational law and the stone's initial height, from which its time of fall and velocity of impact can be deduced. The boiling of water is explained by citing the gas laws and the atmospheric pressure. The period of a pendulum is explained by citing the formula that relates period to length and gravitational acceleration. From these cases one might extract a definition: an account of a phenomenon constitutes a scientific explanation if and only if it deduces the phenomenon from a general law and a statement of initial conditions, the law is a well-established empirical generalisation, and the explanation renders the phenomenon nomically expectable.
>
> But this definition, drawn from the explanatory practices of classical mechanics, proves too restrictive when applied to the historical sciences. Consider the explanation of the extinction of the non-avian dinosaurs at the close of the Cretaceous period. The now widely accepted account holds that a large asteroid struck the earth at what is now the Chicxulub crater in the Yucatan Peninsula, some sixty-six million years ago, and that the environmental consequences of this impact — vast quantities of debris ejected into the atmosphere, a prolonged period of global cooling, the collapse of photosynthesis, and the disruption of food chains — led to the extinction of the dinosaurs and many other species. This explanation is supported by an impressive body of evidence: the worldwide iridium anomaly at the Cretaceous-Palaeogene boundary, the shocked quartz, the tsunami deposits, and the crater itself. Yet it does not deduce the extinction from a general law and initial conditions. There is no law that states what happens whenever an asteroid of a given size strikes a planet; the event is singular and unrepeatable. The explanation does not render the extinction nomically expectable in any deductive sense; rather, it traces the consequences of a particular event through a complex and contingent chain of environmental disruptions. The background cases were all drawn from physics, where deduction from universal laws is the norm. The definition mistook this deductive structure for the essence of all scientific explanation, overlooking the narrative explanations of the historical sciences, in which the aim is not to subsume a phenomenon under a law but to reconstruct the particular sequence of events and processes that brought it about.

*Abductive insight:* The background cases were drawn from classical mechanics, where phenomena are deducible from universal laws and precisely stated initial conditions. The definition abstracted deducibility, well-established law, and nomic expectability as essential features of explanation. But the dinosaur-extinction case reveals that some of the most compelling scientific explanations are historical narratives that trace the consequences of singular events through complex causal chains, without subsuming the explanandum under any general law.

### 2. scientific law

**Definition:** A generalisation constitutes a law of nature if and only if (i) it expresses an exceptionless regularity, (ii) it holds across all times and places, and (iii) it supports counterfactual conditionals — that is, it tells us what would happen under conditions that do not in fact obtain.

> Consider three generalisations that are regarded as laws of nature. Boyle's law states that at constant temperature the pressure of a gas is inversely proportional to its volume — a regularity confirmed by innumerable experiments without known exception. Snell's law states that the ratio of the sines of the angles of incidence and refraction is constant for a given pair of media. Coulomb's law states that the force between two point charges varies as the product of the charges divided by the square of the distance. From these cases one might define a law of nature: a generalisation constitutes a law if and only if it expresses an exceptionless regularity, holds across all times and places, and supports counterfactual conditionals.
>
> But this definition, drawn from the exact physical sciences, encounters difficulty when applied to the laws of biology. Consider Mendel's laws of inheritance. The law of segregation states that the two alleles for a trait separate during gamete formation, so that each gamete carries only one allele. The law of independent assortment states that alleles for different traits are distributed to gametes independently of one another. These generalisations are called laws without hesitation; they are among the most fundamental principles of genetics and they transformed the study of heredity. Yet they are not exceptionless. Genes located on the same chromosome are physically linked and do not assort independently; crossing-over and recombination produce partial exceptions, and phenomena such as meiotic drive and segregation distortion violate the law of segregation itself. These exceptions are well documented, well understood, and widespread. The first condition fails. The background cases were all drawn from physics, where laws describe exact, exceptionless regularities. The definition mistook this exactness for an essential feature of all laws, when in the biological sciences, a generalisation may be recognised as a law even though it admits of systematic exceptions — exceptions that are themselves explicable by further biological mechanisms and that do not diminish the generality or importance of the law itself.

*Abductive insight:* The background cases were all physical laws describing exact, exceptionless regularities. The definition abstracted exceptionlessness, universality, and counterfactual support as essential features. But Mendel's laws reveal that in the biological sciences, laws may describe robust tendencies that admit of systematic exceptions — exceptions that are themselves explicable by further biological principles — without ceasing to be laws.

### 3. theory

**Definition:** A body of propositions constitutes a scientific theory if and only if (i) it is the product of a deliberate programme of theoretical unification, (ii) it has been developed incrementally through the resolution of specific problems, and (iii) it was accepted by the scientific community on the basis of decisive empirical tests.

> Consider three theories. Newton's theory of gravitation unified terrestrial and celestial mechanics through decades of systematic work. Maxwell's electromagnetic theory unified electricity, magnetism, and optics in a single mathematical framework. Einstein's special relativity resolved the tension between Newtonian mechanics and Maxwellian electrodynamics. From these cases one might define a scientific theory: a body of propositions constitutes a theory if and only if it is the product of a deliberate programme of theoretical unification, it has been developed incrementally through the resolution of specific problems, and it was accepted by the scientific community on the basis of decisive empirical tests.
>
> But this definition, drawn from the great theories of mathematical physics, proves too narrow. Consider the theory of plate tectonics. It is one of the most powerful unifying theories in the history of science, explaining the distribution of earthquakes and volcanoes, the formation of mountain ranges, the opening and closing of oceans, and the geographical distribution of fossils. Yet it was not the product of a deliberate programme of theoretical unification. It emerged from disparate lines of inquiry pursued by investigators who were largely unaware of one another's work. Wegener's observations of the fit of the continents, made in the nineteen-tens, were dismissed for decades. Hess's hypothesis of seafloor spreading, proposed in 1962, drew upon oceanographic data that Wegener had never seen. The mapping of magnetic reversals on the ocean floor, carried out by Vine and Matthews, provided evidence that neither Wegener nor Hess had anticipated. And the seismological delineation of plate boundaries was the work of yet another group of researchers. The theory crystallised not through a coordinated programme but through the unexpected convergence of independent discoveries. Nor was it accepted on the basis of a single decisive test; its acceptance was gradual, driven by the cumulative weight of evidence from geology, palaeontology, seismology, and oceanography. The background cases were theories built by deliberate programmes of unification. The definition mistook this deliberateness for an essential feature of all theories, overlooking the possibility that a great unifying theory may emerge unplanned from the convergence of independent lines of evidence.

*Abductive insight:* The background cases were all theories developed by individual geniuses or small groups working within a coherent intellectual programme. The definition abstracted deliberate unification, incremental problem-solving, and decisive testing as essential features. But plate tectonics shows that a great unifying theory may emerge unplanned from the convergence of independent lines of inquiry pursued by researchers who did not set out to construct a unified theory, and may be accepted not on the basis of a single decisive test but through the gradual accumulation of converging evidence.

### 4. classification

**Definition:** A scientific classification is adequate if and only if (i) it is based on a single measurable property of the objects classified, (ii) it assigns each object to exactly one category, and (iii) the boundaries between categories are sharp and non-arbitrary.

> Consider three scientific classifications. The periodic table classifies chemical elements by atomic number, assigning each element a unique position determined by a single measurable property. The classification of geometric figures groups them by the number of sides, with precise and mutually exclusive categories. The classification of crystalline structures assigns each crystal to one of thirty-two point groups determined by its symmetry. From these cases one might define an adequate scientific classification: it must be based on a single measurable property, it must assign each object to exactly one category, and the boundaries between categories must be sharp and non-arbitrary.
>
> But this definition, drawn from cases in which nature provides discrete, sharply bounded categories, proves inadequate when applied to classifications of continuously varying phenomena. Consider the spectral classification of stars, one of the most important taxonomic schemes in astrophysics. Stars are classified into spectral types — the familiar sequence O, B, A, F, G, K, M — on the basis of the pattern of absorption lines in their spectra, which reflects their surface temperature and chemical composition. This classification has been indispensable to the development of modern astronomy; it organises the bewildering variety of stars into a comprehensible sequence and provides the foundation for theories of stellar structure and evolution. Yet it is not based on a single measurable property. Surface temperature is the primary criterion, but luminosity, chemical composition, and the detailed pattern of spectral lines all play a role, and these criteria do not always vary in lockstep. Moreover, the boundaries between spectral types are not sharp. Stars do not fall into discrete natural kinds; they grade continuously from one type to the next, and the dividing lines between O and B, or between G and K, are matters of convention established by the astronomical community. The first and third conditions fail. The background cases were classifications of entities that come in discrete, naturally bounded categories. The definition mistook this discreteness for a universal requirement, overlooking the classifications that impose useful conventional boundaries upon continuous natural variation.

*Abductive insight:* The background cases — the periodic table, geometric figures, crystal symmetry groups — were all classifications with discrete, sharply bounded categories determined by a single criterion. The definition abstracted single-property basis, unique assignment, and sharp boundaries as essential features. But stellar classification reveals that some of the most fruitful scientific classifications deal with continuously varying phenomena, employ multiple criteria, and draw boundaries that are conventional rather than natural, without thereby being arbitrary or inadequate.

### 5. reduction

**Definition:** A scientific theory T1 is reducible to a theory T2 if and only if (i) the entities posited by T1 are shown to be identical with entities described by T2, (ii) the laws of T1 are derivable from the laws of T2, and (iii) T1 becomes explanatorily superfluous once the reduction is complete — that is, everything T1 explains can be explained at least as well by T2 alone.

> Consider three cases of reduction. The laws of geometrical optics are derived from the wave theory of light in the short-wavelength limit. Kepler's laws of planetary motion are derived from Newton's law of gravitation. The ideal gas law is derived from the kinetic theory. From these cases one might define reduction: a theory T1 is reducible to T2 if and only if the entities of T1 are identical with entities described by T2, the laws of T1 are derivable from T2, and T1 becomes explanatorily superfluous once the reduction is complete.
>
> But this definition, drawn from reductions within physics, encounters a fundamental difficulty when applied to the relationship between psychology and neuroscience. Consider the psychological concept of a phobia — an intense, irrational fear of a specific object or situation. A phobia is characterised by its intentional content: it is a fear of something. An arachnophobe fears spiders; a claustrophobe fears enclosed spaces. Now, one might suppose that a phobia is identical with a particular neural state. But the same phobia may be realised by different neural configurations in different individuals; the precise pattern of neural activity underlying a fear of spiders may differ from one brain to another, depending upon the person's history, the structure of her neural connections, and countless other factors. The intentional content of the phobia — its being about spiders — is not captured by any single neural description. Furthermore, the psychological laws governing phobias — that exposure to the feared stimulus produces avoidance behaviour, that phobias can be acquired through classical conditioning and extinguished through systematic desensitisation — are not derivable from the laws of neuroscience. These psychological regularities hold across individuals with very different brains, and they are stated in terms that have no direct translation into the vocabulary of neurons and synapses. And psychology does not become superfluous; the psychological level of description captures generalisations that are invisible at the neural level. The background cases were reductions within physics, where entities at one level could be cleanly identified with entities at a lower level. The definition mistook this clean identification for a universal feature of reduction, overlooking the multiple realisability that makes higher-level sciences explanatorily autonomous.

*Abductive insight:* The background cases were all reductions within physics, where the higher-level entities could be straightforwardly identified with lower-level entities and the higher-level laws derived from lower-level laws. The definition abstracted identity, derivability, and superfluity as essential to reduction. But the relationship between psychology and neuroscience reveals that higher-level sciences may describe patterns that are multiply realisable — implemented by different physical substrates in different cases — so that the higher-level entities cannot be identified with particular lower-level entities, the higher-level laws cannot be derived, and the higher-level descriptions remain explanatorily indispensable.
