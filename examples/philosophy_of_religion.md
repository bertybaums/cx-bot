## DefCx Examples

Each example presents a definition as necessary and sufficient conditions, then constructs a scenario that reveals the definition's insufficiency.

### 1. existence of god

**Definition:** An argument establishes the existence of God if and only if (i) it proceeds from premises that are self-evidently true, (ii) its logical form is deductively valid, and (iii) its conclusion asserts the existence of a being of maximal perfection.

> It might be supposed that an argument establishes the existence of God if and only if it proceeds from premises that are self-evidently true, its logical form is deductively valid, and its conclusion asserts the existence of a being of maximal perfection. This definition captures the ontological argument in its classical Anselmian form rather well: one begins with the concept of a being than which nothing greater can be conceived, and one purports to show by purely a priori reasoning that such a being must exist. Yet the definition excludes what many have taken to be the most persuasive class of theistic arguments. Consider the argument from design. Its premises are drawn from observation of the natural world — the intricate adaptation of organisms to their environments, the regularity of the celestial motions, the apparent purposiveness of natural processes. These premises are not self-evidently true; they rest upon experience and upon the analogical inference that design implies a designer. The first condition fails. Yet the design argument has occupied a central place in natural theology since antiquity, and many have found it more convincing than any purely a priori demonstration. The definition has modelled theistic argumentation upon a single type — the ontological — and has thereby excluded the broad tradition of a posteriori reasoning that constitutes the larger part of the philosophical case for God's existence.

*Missing condition:* The definition restricts genuine theistic arguments to those with self-evident premises, thereby excluding the a posteriori arguments — cosmological, teleological, and experiential — that constitute the greater part of natural theology.

### 2. existence of god

**Definition:** A cosmological argument for God's existence is sound if and only if (i) it demonstrates that the universe had a temporal beginning, (ii) it shows that whatever begins to exist has a cause, and (iii) it identifies that cause as God.

> One might hold that a cosmological argument for God's existence is sound if and only if it demonstrates that the universe had a temporal beginning, it shows that whatever begins to exist has a cause, and it identifies that cause as God. This formulation captures the structure of the kalam cosmological argument tolerably well: the universe began to exist; whatever begins to exist has a cause; therefore the universe has a cause, which is God. Yet it excludes a venerable and distinct tradition. The cosmological argument as developed by Aquinas in the Summa Theologiae does not require the universe to have had a beginning in time. Aquinas was careful to note that reason alone cannot demonstrate whether the world is eternal or temporally finite; he accepted the temporal creation of the world on the authority of revelation, not of philosophy. His argument proceeds instead from the observation that existing things are contingent — they need not exist — to the conclusion that there must be a necessary being upon which all contingent things depend for their existence at every moment. The first condition of the definition fails entirely for this Thomistic formulation. The definition has taken one species of cosmological argument — the kalam — for the whole genus, and has thereby excluded arguments from contingency that form the backbone of Thomistic natural theology.

*Missing condition:* The definition assumes that all cosmological arguments rest upon a temporal beginning, but an important class of such arguments proceeds from the contingency of the world rather than from its finitude in time.

### 3. problem of evil

**Definition:** The problem of evil is a decisive objection to theism if and only if (i) it demonstrates a logical contradiction between the existence of God and the existence of any evil, (ii) the contradiction holds regardless of what reasons God might have, and (iii) the argument is purely deductive.

> It is sometimes supposed that the problem of evil constitutes a decisive objection to theism if and only if it demonstrates a logical contradiction between the existence of God and the existence of any evil, the contradiction holds regardless of what reasons God might have for permitting evil, and the argument is purely deductive. This characterisation fits the logical problem of evil as formulated by certain twentieth-century philosophers: an omnipotent, omniscient, and perfectly good being would eliminate all evil; evil exists; therefore no such being exists. Yet this is not the only form of the problem. Consider the evidential problem of evil. Its proponent does not claim that God and evil are logically incompatible. He concedes that it is logically possible for God to have morally sufficient reasons for permitting some evil. What he argues is that the sheer quantity, intensity, and seemingly pointless distribution of suffering in the world — the prolonged agony of animals in forest fires, the suffering of children from disease — makes it improbable that such reasons exist. The argument is probabilistic rather than deductive, and it does not purport to establish a logical contradiction. The first and third conditions fail. Yet the evidential problem is widely considered the more pressing challenge to theism in contemporary discussion. The definition has identified the problem of evil with its logical version alone, overlooking the probabilistic formulation that many regard as the stronger objection.

*Missing condition:* The definition restricts the problem of evil to its logical form, ignoring the evidential or probabilistic version, which concedes logical possibility but presses the case that observed suffering renders theism unlikely.

### 4. problem of evil

**Definition:** A theodicy is adequate if and only if (i) it explains every instance of suffering by reference to a greater good, (ii) the greater good in question is one that could not have been achieved without the suffering, and (iii) the explanation is intelligible to the sufferer.

> One might propose that a theodicy is adequate if and only if it explains every instance of suffering by reference to a greater good, the greater good could not have been achieved without the suffering, and the explanation is intelligible to the sufferer. This definition sets a high standard: each particular evil must be accounted for, and the account must make sense to those who endure it. Yet the most influential theodicies in the tradition fall short of this standard. Consider the soul-making theodicy, associated with Irenaeus and developed in modern form by John Hick. It holds that suffering provides the conditions necessary for the development of moral and spiritual virtues — courage, patience, compassion, fortitude — and that a world without suffering would be a world without the possibility of such growth. This is a powerful general account. But it does not explain every particular instance of suffering. The death of an infant from a painful disease does not contribute to the moral development of the infant, who is too young to develop virtues of any kind. The first condition fails for such cases. Furthermore, the explanation is frequently unintelligible or even deeply offensive to those in the midst of suffering; to tell a bereaved parent that her child's death served a higher purpose may strike her as callous rather than illuminating. The third condition fails. Yet the soul-making theodicy remains one of the most carefully developed responses to the problem of evil. The definition has demanded too much specificity and too much transparency from a project that, by its nature, operates at the level of general principle.

*Missing condition:* The definition demands that a theodicy explain every individual case and be comprehensible to the afflicted, but the most respected theodicies offer general frameworks rather than case-by-case explanations, and they acknowledge that the divine purposes may exceed human understanding.

### 5. miracle

**Definition:** An event is a miracle if and only if (i) it is a violation of a law of nature, (ii) it is brought about by the direct intervention of God, and (iii) it serves a discernible religious purpose.

> A familiar account holds that an event is a miracle if and only if it is a violation of a law of nature, it is brought about by the direct intervention of God, and it serves a discernible religious purpose. This definition, which owes much to Hume's formulation, captures the dramatic biblical miracles tolerably well: the parting of the Red Sea, the raising of Lazarus, the turning of water into wine. Each appears to involve the suspension or violation of a natural regularity by divine power for a manifest religious end. Yet the definition proves too narrow for the broader class of events that religious communities regard as miraculous. Consider a man who has been diagnosed with a terminal illness and given weeks to live. His congregation prays fervently on his behalf. Against all medical expectation, he recovers completely. His physicians can offer no explanation, though they do not say that any law of nature has been violated — only that the recovery is extraordinarily improbable. Many in the congregation call the event a miracle, and they regard it as a sign of divine favour. The first condition fails: no law of nature has been violated. The event is merely supremely improbable and providentially timed. Yet it is treated as miraculous. The definition has insisted upon a strict violation of natural law, when in practice the concept of miracle extends to remarkable coincidences and improbable events whose significance is discerned by the faithful.

*Missing condition:* The definition requires a violation of natural law, but many events regarded as miraculous are not violations of law but rather extraordinarily improbable occurrences whose timing and circumstances suggest providential significance.

## AbdCx Examples

Each example presents background cases that motivate a definition, then constructs a scenario revealing what the background cases shared but the definition missed.

### 1. existence of god

**Definition:** An argument for God's existence is compelling if and only if (i) it proceeds from uncontroversial empirical premises, (ii) the inference to a divine cause is the only plausible explanation, and (iii) the conclusion specifies the attributes of the God whose existence is proved.

> Consider three arguments for the existence of God. The cosmological argument begins from the existence of the universe and infers a first cause. The teleological argument begins from the apparent design of organisms and infers a designer. The moral argument begins from the objectivity of moral obligation and infers a transcendent moral ground. Each proceeds from empirical observation — of the world's existence, its order, its moral structure — to the conclusion that God exists. From these cases one might define a compelling theistic argument: it proceeds from uncontroversial empirical premises, the inference to a divine cause is the only plausible explanation, and the conclusion specifies the attributes of the God whose existence is proved.
>
> But this definition, drawn from a posteriori arguments, proves too narrow. Consider the ontological argument, first formulated by Anselm in the Proslogion. Anselm begins not from any empirical observation but from the concept of God as a being than which nothing greater can be conceived. He argues that such a being must exist in reality as well as in the understanding, for a being that exists only in the understanding is not truly the greatest conceivable. The argument proceeds entirely a priori; its premises are conceptual, not empirical. The first condition fails. Yet the ontological argument has been taken seriously by philosophers of the first rank, including Descartes, Leibniz, and in modified form Plantinga. The background cases were all a posteriori arguments; the definition took their empirical character as essential, when the ontological argument demonstrates that a purely conceptual route to the existence of God has been and continues to be vigorously pursued.

*Abductive insight:* The background cases were all a posteriori arguments beginning from observed features of the world. The definition abstracted the empirical starting-point as essential, but the ontological argument shows that a purely a priori route to God's existence is available, proceeding from conceptual analysis rather than from experience.

### 2. problem of evil

**Definition:** A theodicy adequately addresses the problem of evil if and only if (i) it identifies a morally sufficient reason for each instance of suffering, (ii) the reason is one that the sufferer could in principle recognise, and (iii) the suffering is proportionate to the good it produces.

> Consider three cases of suffering and their theodical explanations. A man loses his livelihood in a flood; the theodicist says the loss tests and deepens his faith. A woman endures prolonged illness; the theodicist says the illness cultivates patience and compassion. A community suffers famine; the theodicist says the famine calls forth charity and solidarity. From these cases one might define an adequate theodicy: it identifies a morally sufficient reason for each instance of suffering, the reason is one the sufferer could in principle recognise, and the suffering is proportionate to the good it produces.
>
> But this definition, drawn from cases of human suffering that admit of moral or spiritual interpretation, proves inadequate when extended beyond the human sphere. Consider the suffering of a fawn trapped in a forest fire. The animal lies in agony for days before dying. It is not a moral agent; it cannot develop patience, deepen its faith, or learn charity from its ordeal. There is no morally sufficient reason that the fawn could recognise, for recognition of moral reasons requires capacities the fawn does not possess. The first and second conditions fail entirely. Yet the suffering of animals is not a marginal phenomenon; it is a vast and pervasive feature of the natural world. The background cases were all anthropocentric — they involved human beings who could be imagined to benefit from their trials. The definition mistook this anthropocentric sample for the full range of suffering and constructed a theodicy that has nothing to say about the agony of creatures that lie beyond the reach of moral interpretation.

*Abductive insight:* The background cases all involved human sufferers who could, in principle, derive moral or spiritual benefit from their trials. The definition assumed that all suffering has a recognisable purpose for the sufferer, but animal suffering falls outside this framework entirely, revealing that the theodicy was built upon an anthropocentric sample.

### 3. miracle

**Definition:** An event is credibly miraculous if and only if (i) it is sudden and dramatic, (ii) it is witnessed by a crowd, and (iii) it admits of no natural explanation at the time of its occurrence.

> Consider three cases of miracle. A paralysed man rises and walks after a saint lays hands upon him; the event is instantaneous and witnessed by bystanders. A spring of water appears in a barren desert at the prayer of a holy man; the event is sudden and public. A storm ceases at the command of a prophet; those aboard the vessel observe the transformation. From these cases one might define a credible miracle: the event is sudden and dramatic, it is witnessed by a crowd, and it admits of no natural explanation at the time of its occurrence.
>
> But this definition, drawn from spectacular public events, proves too restrictive. Consider the phenomenon of incorruption: the preservation of a saint's body from decomposition long after death. The body of Bernadette of Lourdes, for instance, was exhumed decades after her death and found remarkably intact. The phenomenon is not sudden — it unfolds over years and decades. It is not dramatic in any theatrical sense; the body simply lies in its reliquary, undecayed. And it is not witnessed by a crowd at a single moment; successive visitors observe it over a period of generations. The first and second conditions fail. Yet incorruption is widely treated as a miracle by the Catholic Church and other traditions. The background cases were all instantaneous public spectacles; the definition took their suddenness and public character as essential. But the miraculous, as the traditions understand it, may manifest itself quietly and gradually, disclosing itself over time rather than in a single dramatic moment.

*Abductive insight:* The background cases were all instantaneous public spectacles. The definition abstracted suddenness and public witness as essential features, but many putative miracles are quiet, gradual, and observed over extended periods rather than in a single dramatic moment.

### 4. faith

**Definition:** The adoption of religious faith is rational if and only if (i) it is preceded by a deliberate investigation of the evidence, (ii) the evidence is weighed by the canons of inductive reasoning, and (iii) the believer can articulate the evidential basis of his faith to others.

> Consider three paths to faith. A man reads the philosophical arguments for God's existence and, finding them persuasive, adopts a religious faith on their basis. A woman studies the historical evidence for scriptural events, judges it reliable, and comes to believe. A scholar weighs the testimony of witnesses to miracles and concludes that faith is warranted. From these cases one might define rational faith: its adoption is preceded by deliberate investigation, the evidence is weighed inductively, and the believer can articulate his evidential basis to others.
>
> But this definition, drawn from cases of adult intellectual conversion, proves too restrictive. Consider a child raised in a devout household. He attends worship with his family, learns the prayers and stories of his tradition, and comes to believe in God not through argument but through immersion in a form of life. He has never read a philosophical proof or weighed historical evidence. He cannot articulate an evidential basis for his faith in the manner of a scholar. The first and second conditions fail. Yet the child's faith is sincere, it shapes his moral development, and many theologians — Catholic, Reformed, and Orthodox alike — would regard it as an entirely fitting and rational response to his circumstances. The background cases were all instances of adult deliberation; the definition treated this investigative path as the only rational route. But faith may be acquired rationally through formation, communal worship, and the gradual internalisation of a tradition, without any antecedent investigation of the kind the definition demands.

*Abductive insight:* The background cases all involved adult enquirers who came to faith through intellectual investigation. The definition treated this investigative path as the only rational route to faith, but faith may be rationally acquired through formation, communal practice, and the gradual absorption of a tradition, without any explicit evidential inquiry.

### 5. sacred

**Definition:** An object acquires sacred status if and only if (i) it undergoes a formal rite of consecration, (ii) its sacred status is recognised by a hierarchical religious institution, and (iii) prohibitions against its misuse are codified in religious law.

> Consider three sacred objects. A cathedral consecrated by a bishop is sacred; elaborate rituals mark its dedication. A Torah scroll prepared according to halakhic law is sacred; special rules govern its care. A relic of a saint, authenticated by ecclesiastical authority, is venerated as sacred. From these cases one might define the acquisition of sacred status: the object undergoes a formal rite of consecration, its status is recognised by a hierarchical religious institution, and prohibitions against misuse are codified in religious law.
>
> But this definition, drawn from institutional religions with formal hierarchies and written codes, fails when applied to traditions of a different structure. Consider the River Ganges. For hundreds of millions of Hindus, the Ganges is supremely sacred — to bathe in its waters is to be purified, and its very name is uttered in prayer. Yet no single rite of consecration was ever performed upon the river. Its sacredness is primordial, rooted in mythology: the river descended from heaven through the matted hair of Shiva. Hinduism does not possess a single hierarchical institution that confers sacred status in the manner of the Catholic episcopacy. Prohibitions concerning the Ganges arise from custom and devotion, not from any codified religious law in the Western sense. All three conditions fail. The background cases were drawn from traditions with bishops, rabbis, and ecclesiastical courts; the definition took institutional machinery as essential. But the Ganges reveals that sacredness may be constituted by communal devotion and living mythology, without formal consecration, hierarchical recognition, or codified prohibition.

*Abductive insight:* The background cases were all drawn from institutional religions with formal hierarchies and codified law. The definition treated institutional recognition as essential to sacredness, but in traditions with decentralised authority and oral custom, sacredness arises from communal devotion and mythological tradition rather than from institutional certification.
