## DefCx Examples

Each example presents a definition as necessary and sufficient conditions, then constructs a scenario that reveals the definition's insufficiency.

### 1. beauty

**Definition:** An object is beautiful if and only if (i) it exhibits symmetry, (ii) its parts are harmoniously arranged, and (iii) it produces pleasure upon contemplation.

> It has been maintained that an object is beautiful if and only if it exhibits symmetry, its parts are harmoniously arranged, and it produces pleasure upon contemplation. This definition, though it captures a familiar class of beautiful objects — Greek temples, certain musical compositions, the proportions of the human face — proves too narrow upon examination. Consider a wild and irregular mountain landscape, with jagged peaks, disordered clouds, and cascading torrents. There is no symmetry to be found, and its parts are not arranged in any relation that might be called harmonious. Yet the scene produces intense pleasure upon contemplation, and we do not hesitate to call it beautiful. The difficulty is instructive. The definition has identified certain formal properties which are sufficient for beauty in some cases, but it has failed to accommodate the beauty of the irregular, the vast, and the sublime. One suspects that beauty is a wider category than the analysis allows, admitting of instances that owe their beauty to grandeur or expressive power rather than to proportion and regularity.

*Missing condition:* Beauty may arise from grandeur or expressive power, not only from formal properties such as symmetry and harmony.

### 2. art

**Definition:** An object is a work of art if and only if (i) it is produced by a human being, (ii) it is produced with the intention to be appreciated aesthetically, and (iii) it belongs to a recognised artistic medium.

> One might define a work of art as an object produced by a human being, with the intention that it be appreciated aesthetically, and belonging to a recognised artistic medium such as painting, sculpture, or music. Yet this definition admits of a troublesome case. Consider a forger of exceptional skill who produces a painting indistinguishable from a masterwork by Vermeer. The forgery is produced by a human being, it is produced with the clear intention that it be appreciated aesthetically — the forger, after all, desires that viewers admire it — and it belongs to the recognised medium of painting. The three conditions are satisfied in full. Yet when the forgery is discovered, we feel strongly that it is not a genuine work of art, or at any rate not a work of art in the same sense as the original. Something is missing from the definition: perhaps the requirement that the object be an original expression, embodying the creative vision of its maker rather than merely replicating the achievement of another.

*Missing condition:* The object must be an original expression, not a mere replication of another's creative achievement.

### 3. taste

**Definition:** A person has good taste if and only if (i) he can discriminate fine differences in aesthetic quality, (ii) his judgements are consistent over time, and (iii) his judgements accord with those of other persons of acknowledged taste.

> It is sometimes supposed that a person has good taste if and only if he can discriminate fine differences in aesthetic quality, his judgements are consistent over time, and his judgements accord with those of other persons of acknowledged taste. This analysis, however, proves vulnerable to the following case. Imagine a man who devotes himself to the appreciation of popular sentimental paintings — scenes of weeping children, sunsets over cottages, and the like. Within this genre, he discriminates with extraordinary fineness: he can detect the slightest variation in colouring, composition, and emotional tone. His judgements are perfectly consistent over time, and they accord with those of a large and enthusiastic community of like-minded collectors, each of whom regards the others as persons of taste. The three conditions are satisfied. Yet we should hesitate to say that this man has good taste. His discrimination, though genuine, is exercised upon objects of little aesthetic worth. The definition has failed to distinguish between mere consistency of judgement and the capacity to recognise genuine aesthetic excellence.

*Missing condition:* The objects of judgement must be of genuine aesthetic merit, not merely popular or fashionable.

### 4. aesthetic experience

**Definition:** An experience is aesthetic if and only if (i) attention is directed to the formal properties of an object, (ii) the contemplation is disinterested, and (iii) the experience is accompanied by pleasure or displeasure.

> The view that an experience is aesthetic if and only if attention is directed to the formal properties of an object, the contemplation is disinterested, and the experience is accompanied by pleasure or displeasure has a distinguished pedigree. Yet it admits of a troublesome case. Suppose a jewel thief, while appraising a diamond he intends to steal, finds himself attending to the formal properties of the stone — its cut, its symmetry, the play of light through its facets. His contemplation is, in a sense, disinterested: he is not at that moment thinking of the price he will obtain, but simply looking at the stone. And the experience is accompanied by pleasure. The three conditions appear to be satisfied. Yet we should be reluctant to call this an aesthetic experience in the proper sense, for the pleasure is deeply entangled with the thief's awareness of the diamond's value and his anticipation of possessing it. The definition, it seems, fails to require that the pleasure arise from the formal properties themselves, rather than from extraneous associations.

*Missing condition:* The pleasure must arise from the formal properties themselves, not from associations with the object's monetary or practical value.

### 5. expression

**Definition:** A work of art is expressive of an emotion if and only if (i) the artist experienced the emotion during creation, (ii) the work evokes the same emotion in the audience, and (iii) the work contains formal properties associated with that emotion.

> A natural theory of artistic expression holds that a work of art is expressive of an emotion if and only if the artist experienced the emotion during creation, the work evokes the same emotion in the audience, and the work contains formal properties associated with that emotion. But consider the following case. A poet, in a state of perfect contentment, sits down to compose a poem about grief. He is a craftsman of great skill, and he deploys the formal properties associated with sorrow — slow rhythms, descending cadences, images of loss — with consummate art. The resulting poem reliably evokes deep sorrow in its readers. Two of the three conditions are satisfied: the work evokes sorrow and contains the appropriate formal properties. But the first condition fails entirely, for the poet experienced no sorrow whatever during the act of composition. Yet we should not hesitate to say that the poem is expressive of sorrow. The definition, it appears, has confused the biographical circumstances of creation with the expressive character of the work itself.

*Missing condition:* Expressiveness may depend on the formal properties and their effect alone, without requiring that the artist actually experienced the emotion.

## AbdCx Examples

Each example presents background cases that motivate a definition, then constructs a scenario revealing what the background cases shared but the definition missed.

### 1. representation

**Definition:** A painting represents an object if and only if (i) it visually resembles the object, (ii) the artist intended to depict that object, and (iii) a competent viewer can identify the object from the painting.

> Consider three cases of pictorial representation. A portrait of a duke, painted with great skill, represents the duke and is valued as art. A landscape of the Alps represents those mountains and is admired for its fidelity. A still life of fruit upon a table represents those objects and is prized for its craftsmanship. From these cases one might extract a definition: a painting represents an object if and only if it visually resembles the object, the artist intended to depict it, and a competent viewer can identify the object from the painting.
>
> Yet this analysis, drawn from naturalistic painting, proves too narrow. Consider a cubist painting of a guitar. The artist intended to depict a guitar, and a viewer familiar with the conventions of cubism can identify the subject. But the painting does not visually resemble any guitar in the ordinary sense: the forms are fragmented, the perspective is multiple, and the shapes bear little likeness to the instrument as it appears to the naked eye. The first condition fails, yet the painting plainly represents a guitar. Our background cases were all naturalistic works in which resemblance was straightforward; the definition mistook this incidental feature for an essential one. Representation, it appears, may proceed through convention and stylistic practice, not only through visual resemblance.

*Abductive insight:* The background cases all involved naturalistic painting where resemblance was straightforward. The definition treated resemblance as necessary, but representation can be achieved through convention and style without visual similarity.

### 2. sublimity

**Definition:** An object is sublime if and only if (i) it is vast or powerful beyond ordinary measure, (ii) it overwhelms the senses, and (iii) it produces a mixture of awe and terror.

> Consider the following experiences of the sublime. A vast mountain range, viewed from a great distance, inspires awe and a sense of one's own insignificance. A violent thunderstorm over the open sea produces a mixture of terror and exhilaration. An immense waterfall, heard before it is seen, overwhelms the senses with its power. From these cases one might define the sublime: an object is sublime if and only if it is vast or powerful beyond ordinary measure, it overwhelms the senses, and it produces a mixture of awe and terror.
>
> But this definition, drawn from dramatic natural phenomena, proves too narrow. Consider a perfectly clear night sky, viewed from a place far from any artificial light. The stars are innumerable, the darkness is infinite, and the observer is filled with profound awe. Yet the sky does not overwhelm the senses in the manner of a thunderstorm or a waterfall — it is perfectly quiet and still. And it produces no terror, only a serene and contemplative wonder. The second and third conditions fail, yet the experience is unmistakably one of sublimity. The background cases were all instances of dynamic, threatening grandeur; the definition captured what those cases had in common but missed the sublimity of the infinite and the still, which is no less real for being quiet.

*Abductive insight:* The background cases involved dynamic, threatening natural phenomena. The definition captured the overwhelming and terrifying character of these particular examples but missed the quiet sublimity of the infinite and the still.

### 3. elegance

**Definition:** A work or performance is elegant if and only if (i) it achieves a significant result, (ii) it does so with the least possible expenditure of means, and (iii) the economy of means is perceptible to the observer.

> Consider three cases of elegance. Euclid's proof that there are infinitely many prime numbers achieves a momentous result with the barest minimum of apparatus, and we call it elegant. A Georgian terrace produces an impression of grandeur through the simplest means — brick, proportion, repetition — and is admired for its elegance. A Shakespearean sonnet conveys profound feeling within the tightest formal constraints, and its elegance is universally acknowledged. From these cases one might extract a definition: a work or performance is elegant if and only if it achieves a significant result, does so with the least possible expenditure of means, and the economy of means is perceptible to the observer.
>
> Yet this analysis admits an unwelcome counterexample. Consider a factory-produced wooden crate, designed for the conveyance of goods. It achieves its purpose — the safe transport of heavy objects — with a strict minimum of material; not a plank is wasted. The economy is plainly perceptible to anyone who inspects the crate. All three conditions are satisfied. Yet no one would call a shipping crate elegant. The background cases — Euclid's proof, the Georgian terrace, the sonnet — all belonged to domains in which aesthetic appreciation is invited and expected. The definition captured the structural feature of economy but dropped the requirement that the economy be exercised within a context that invites aesthetic regard — that the achievement be one in which the manner of accomplishment is itself an object of contemplation, not merely a means to a practical end.

*Abductive insight:* The background cases all involved works whose economy was exercised within a domain where aesthetic appreciation is expected — mathematics, architecture, poetry. The definition captured economy and significance but dropped the implicit requirement that the economy be displayed within a context that invites aesthetic regard.

### 4. originality

**Definition:** A work of art is original if and only if (i) it departs from the conventions governing its genre or medium at the time of its production, (ii) the departure is the deliberate product of the artist's creative choice, and (iii) the departure introduces elements that were not present in prior works within the same tradition.

> Consider three cases of artistic originality. Turner's late seascapes abandoned conventional draughtsmanship for swirling colour and light; we call them strikingly original. Beethoven's late quartets broke decisively with the forms of his predecessors, and their originality is universally acknowledged. Sterne's Tristram Shandy defied the conventions of the novel in its very structure; its originality remains remarkable to this day. From these cases one might define originality: a work is original if and only if it departs from the conventions governing its genre at the time of its production, the departure is the deliberate product of the artist's creative choice, and it introduces elements not present in prior works within the same tradition.
>
> Yet this analysis admits a troublesome counterexample. Consider an incompetent painter who, entirely ignorant of the conventions of portraiture, produces a canvas that departs wildly from every established norm. The eyes are at different levels, the colours bear no relation to nature, and the composition defies every principle of arrangement. The departure is deliberate — the painter paints exactly as he wishes — and the elements are novel; nothing quite like this has appeared before. All three conditions are satisfied. Yet we should not call the result original; it is merely inept. Turner, Beethoven, and Sterne all commanded the traditions from which they departed; their originality was exercised from a position of mastery. The definition captured the fact of departure but omitted the crucial requirement that the artist possess a deep command of the conventions he transgresses — that originality presuppose, rather than merely negate, the tradition.

*Abductive insight:* The background cases all involved masters who departed from conventions they had first thoroughly absorbed. The definition captured departure and novelty but omitted the requirement that the artist command the tradition from which he departs — that originality presuppose mastery.

### 5. harmony

**Definition:** A composition is harmonious if and only if (i) it consists of distinguishable parts, (ii) the parts are so arranged that none seems superfluous or lacking, and (iii) the arrangement yields a unified perceptual impression.

> Consider three cases of what we call harmony. A well-tuned string quartet blends four distinct voices into a unified sound; we praise its harmony. A Renaissance altarpiece unites vivid colours, contrasting figures, and architectural framing into a coherent whole; its harmony is admired. A formal garden reconciles geometry and nature, hedgerow and open lawn, shade and sunlight; we call it harmonious. From these cases one might extract a definition: a composition is harmonious if and only if it consists of distinguishable parts, the parts are so arranged that none seems superfluous or lacking, and the arrangement yields a unified perceptual impression.
>
> Yet consider a military parade. It consists of clearly distinguishable units — infantry, cavalry, artillery — arranged so that each has its proper place and none seems superfluous. The whole produces a powerfully unified impression of order and precision. All three conditions are satisfied. Yet one might observe that the parade is not harmonious in the aesthetic sense; it is regimented. The unity of the parade arises not from the internal sympathies of its parts but from external command — each unit marches in step because it is ordered to, not because its movement naturally complements that of the others. In the background cases — the quartet, the altarpiece, the garden — unity arose from mutual responsiveness: voices answered one another, colours complemented their neighbours, hedgerow and lawn each set the other off. The definition captured the fact of unity but failed to require that the unity be organic — that it arise from the reciprocal relations of the parts rather than from an external principle of order imposed upon them.

*Abductive insight:* The background cases all involved compositions in which the unity arose from the internal sympathies of the parts — voices blending, colours complementing, forms answering one another. The definition captured multiplicity and unity but omitted the requirement that the unity arise organically from mutual responsiveness, not from external imposition.
