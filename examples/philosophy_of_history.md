## DefCx Examples

Each example presents a definition as necessary and sufficient conditions, then constructs a scenario that reveals the definition's insufficiency.

### 1. progress

**Definition:** A civilisation exhibits progress if and only if (i) each successive generation possesses more material wealth than the preceding one, (ii) the stock of scientific and technical understanding increases cumulatively, and (iii) the members of the civilisation themselves regard their condition as an improvement upon that of their forebears.

> One might propose that a civilisation exhibits progress if and only if each successive generation possesses more material wealth than the preceding one, the stock of scientific and technical understanding increases cumulatively, and the members of the civilisation themselves regard their condition as an improvement upon that of their forebears. This definition captures certain paradigmatic cases of progress: the expansion of European industry in the nineteenth century, during which wealth multiplied, scientific discovery accelerated, and a prevailing optimism about the future took hold among the educated classes. Yet the definition proves inadequate when one considers the transition from the Roman Republic to the early Imperial period. Under Augustus and his successors, the material wealth of the Roman world increased enormously. Trade flourished across the Mediterranean; the engineering of roads, aqueducts, and public buildings reached unprecedented sophistication; and the population of the capital grew to a size unmatched in the ancient world. The first two conditions are amply satisfied. But the Romans themselves were far from unanimous in regarding their condition as improved. Tacitus wrote with bitter nostalgia of the old republican virtues — the frugality, the martial discipline, the independence of spirit — which he believed had been corroded by imperial luxury. Sallust lamented the moral corruption that had accompanied the growth of wealth. Juvenal satirised the decadence of a city that had traded its liberties for bread and circuses. The third condition fails. The definition has assumed that material and technical advancement must be accompanied by a subjective sense of betterment, when in truth a civilisation may advance in measurable respects whilst its most articulate members insist that it has declined.

*Missing condition:* The definition requires that the members of the civilisation regard their situation as improved, but progress in material and technical domains may coincide with a widespread perception of moral or cultural decline, so that progress and the consciousness of progress need not accompany one another.

### 2. historical explanation

**Definition:** An account constitutes an historical explanation if and only if (i) it identifies a set of antecedent conditions that obtained prior to the event, (ii) it subsumes the event under a general law connecting such conditions to outcomes of that type, and (iii) the explanation is such that, given the antecedent conditions and the law, the event could have been predicted in advance.

> It has been maintained that an account constitutes an historical explanation if and only if it identifies a set of antecedent conditions that obtained prior to the event in question, it subsumes the event under a general law connecting such conditions to outcomes of that type, and the explanation is such that, given the antecedent conditions and the law, the event could have been predicted in advance. This is the covering-law model applied to historical inquiry, and it appears to work tolerably well for certain regularities: the collapse of a bridge may be explained by the antecedent conditions of its loading and the laws of structural mechanics. Yet consider the explanation a competent historian offers for the outbreak of the French Revolution. He traces the fiscal crisis of the Bourbon monarchy, the failure of Calonne and Necker to achieve reform, the summoning of the Estates-General, the intransigence of the privileged orders, the popular fury of July 1789, and the fall of the Bastille. This narrative is detailed, persuasive, and widely regarded as explanatory. But it does not subsume the Revolution under any general law. There is no law of the form 'whenever a monarchy faces fiscal crisis and the aristocracy resists reform, revolution follows,' for many monarchies have faced precisely those conditions without revolution resulting. Nor could the Revolution have been predicted from the antecedent conditions; as late as the spring of 1789, few if any observers foresaw the events of the summer. The second and third conditions fail. The definition has imported from the natural sciences a model of explanation that does not fit the practice of historical inquiry, where explanation characteristically proceeds by tracing a particular causal narrative rather than by deducing events from covering laws.

*Missing condition:* The definition demands subsumption under general laws and predictability, but historical explanation frequently proceeds by tracing a particular causal narrative — showing how one event led to another in the specific circumstances — without invoking any law from which the outcome could have been deduced.

### 3. periodisation

**Definition:** A division of the past into periods is legitimate if and only if (i) the boundaries between periods correspond to abrupt and decisive changes in the political order, (ii) the events within each period share a recognisable unity of character, and (iii) the transition from one period to the next is acknowledged by contemporaries as a rupture.

> One might hold that a division of the past into periods is legitimate if and only if the boundaries between periods correspond to abrupt and decisive changes in the political order, the events within each period share a recognisable unity of character, and the transition from one period to the next is acknowledged by contemporaries as a rupture. This definition seems plausible when applied to certain cases. The fall of the Roman Empire in the West, conventionally dated to 476, marks a boundary between antiquity and the mediaeval period; it was a dramatic political event, and something like a new character of European life did emerge in its aftermath. Yet the definition encounters serious difficulty with the transition from the mediaeval to the early modern period, conventionally placed around 1500. No single abrupt political change marks this boundary. The decline of feudal arrangements was gradual, extending over centuries. The rise of centralised monarchies was a slow process visible in retrospect but scarcely perceptible from year to year. The spread of printing, the voyages of discovery, the stirring of new intellectual currents — all these developed over decades rather than erupting in a single upheaval. Nor did contemporaries recognise a rupture. A scholar in Paris in 1510 would not have supposed himself to be living at the dawn of a new age; he would have felt continuity with the intellectual traditions of the preceding centuries. The first and third conditions fail. Yet the division between mediaeval and early modern is one of the most firmly established in historical scholarship. The definition has demanded political drama and contemporary consciousness of change, when the most enduring periodisations frequently correspond to transformations so gradual that only later generations can discern them.

*Missing condition:* The definition requires that periodisation be grounded in abrupt political change and contemporary recognition of rupture, but many of the most widely accepted periodisations correspond to gradual transformations in culture, economy, and thought that were invisible to those who lived through them.

### 4. civilisation

**Definition:** A society constitutes a civilisation if and only if (i) it possesses a system of writing, (ii) it has developed monumental architecture, and (iii) it maintains a specialised priestly or scholarly class devoted to the preservation and transmission of learning.

> It is sometimes proposed that a society constitutes a civilisation if and only if it possesses a system of writing, it has developed monumental architecture, and it maintains a specialised priestly or scholarly class devoted to the preservation and transmission of learning. This definition captures the great civilisations of the ancient Near East admirably. Mesopotamia developed cuneiform, Egypt developed hieroglyphics, and both produced monumental temples and a learned scribal class. The three conditions are satisfied, and the designation of these societies as civilisations commands universal assent. Yet the definition proves too restrictive when one turns to the Americas. The Inca Empire was, by any reasonable measure, one of the great civilisations of the pre-Columbian world. It governed a territory of extraordinary extent along the spine of the Andes, from modern Ecuador to Chile. It built Machu Picchu, Sacsayhuamán, and a road network rivalling that of Rome. It maintained a complex administrative apparatus, a sophisticated agricultural system of terraced farming, and a hierarchical society with a specialised ruling and priestly class. Yet the Inca did not possess a system of writing in the conventional sense. They employed quipu — knotted strings of varying colours and lengths — to record numerical and possibly narrative information, but this falls outside what the definition means by writing. The first condition fails. The definition has taken a feature characteristic of the literate civilisations of Eurasia and imposed it as a universal requirement, when a society may achieve the organisational complexity and cultural accomplishment that merit the designation of civilisation without having developed writing as such.

*Missing condition:* The definition equates civilisation with literacy, but a society may achieve the organisational complexity, monumental construction, and cultural sophistication characteristic of civilisation without developing a system of writing, employing instead other means of recording and transmitting information.

### 5. decline

**Definition:** A civilisation is in decline if and only if (i) its military power diminishes relative to its rivals, (ii) its territory contracts, and (iii) its population decreases over a sustained period.

> One might define decline as follows: a civilisation is in decline if and only if its military power diminishes relative to its rivals, its territory contracts, and its population decreases over a sustained period. This definition appears to fit certain well-known cases. The Western Roman Empire in its final centuries suffered military reverses, lost province after province, and saw the population of its cities dwindle. All three conditions obtain, and the diagnosis of decline is uncontroversial. Yet consider the Ottoman Empire in the eighteenth century. It is a commonplace of historical scholarship that the Ottoman state was in decline during this period — that the vigour and administrative competence of the classical age had given way to corruption, provincial fragmentation, and institutional rigidity. The Janissary corps had degenerated from an elite fighting force into a self-serving political faction. Provincial governors acted with increasing independence from the capital. The cultural and intellectual life of the empire, once magnificent, had lost much of its former energy. Yet the Ottoman territory, though somewhat reduced by the Treaty of Karlowitz and subsequent agreements, remained enormous, stretching from the Balkans to Mesopotamia and from the Crimea to North Africa. And the Ottoman population was not decreasing; demographic evidence suggests that it was growing throughout much of the century. The second condition is only imperfectly satisfied and the third fails altogether. The definition has equated decline with measurable contraction — fewer soldiers, less territory, fewer people — when a civilisation may decline through the quieter processes of institutional decay, administrative dysfunction, and the slow ebbing of cultural confidence.

*Missing condition:* The definition locates decline exclusively in measurable indices of military, territorial, and demographic contraction, but a civilisation may be in decline by virtue of institutional decay, administrative dysfunction, and loss of cultural vitality even whilst its territory remains extensive and its population grows.

## AbdCx Examples

Each example presents background cases that motivate a definition, then constructs a scenario revealing what the background cases shared but the definition missed.

### 1. progress

**Definition:** A society has progressed if and only if (i) the material conditions of life for the majority of its members have measurably improved, (ii) the improvements are the result of deliberate human effort and planning, and (iii) the improvements are irreversible under normal circumstances.

> Consider three cases commonly cited as instances of progress. The development of sanitation and public health in Victorian Britain, undertaken through deliberate legislation and municipal planning, which reduced infant mortality and extended life expectancy. The spread of literacy in Prussia following the introduction of compulsory schooling, a policy deliberately adopted by the state. The expansion of the American railway network, planned and financed by a combination of government land grants and private enterprise, which opened a continent to settlement and trade. From these cases one might extract a definition: a society has progressed if and only if the material conditions of life for the majority of its members have measurably improved, the improvements are the result of deliberate human effort and planning, and the improvements are irreversible under normal circumstances.
>
> But this definition, drawn from cases of planned improvement in societies that broadly benefited, proves too restrictive when one considers the ecological and demographic transformation of Australia following British colonisation. For the European settlers, the material improvement was enormous. Within a few generations, a pastoral and agricultural economy of considerable prosperity had been established across vast territories. Towns, roads, and ports were built; living standards rose; the population grew rapidly. Yet these improvements were not the product of deliberate planning directed at progress. They were the largely uncoordinated consequences of thousands of individual settlers pursuing immediate economic advantage — claiming land, running sheep, clearing forests — without any overarching design. The transformation was as unplanned as it was consequential. Moreover, what constituted progress for the settlers constituted catastrophe for the indigenous population, whose lands were taken, whose societies were disrupted, and whose numbers were devastated. The background cases were all instances where planned effort yielded broad benefit. The definition mistook this conjunction of planning and benefit for an essential feature of progress, overlooking the possibility that material improvement may emerge without deliberate design, and that what counts as progress depends upon whose conditions one measures.

*Abductive insight:* The background cases — Victorian public health, Prussian education, American railways — were all instances of planned, state-directed improvement with broadly beneficial results. The definition abstracted deliberate planning as essential to progress, but the Australian case reveals that large-scale material improvement may emerge from dispersed, uncoordinated actions without any overarching plan, and that such improvement may be described as progress by some whilst constituting catastrophe for others.

### 2. historical explanation

**Definition:** An historical explanation is satisfactory if and only if (i) it identifies the principal agents whose decisions shaped the outcome, (ii) it shows how those decisions interacted with the circumstances to produce the event, and (iii) the explanation would be accepted as adequate by a competent contemporary observer who possessed all the relevant facts.

> Consider three explanatory successes in military history. The historian explains the defeat of the Spanish Armada by citing English tactical superiority, the disruption at Gravelines, and the storms. The fall of Constantinople is explained by Ottoman siege preparations, Byzantine weakness, and the breach in the walls. The Norman Conquest is explained by William's preparations, Harold's exhaustion, and the cavalry at Hastings. In each case the historian identifies the agents whose decisions proved decisive and shows how those decisions, interacting with the circumstances, produced the outcome. From these cases one might define satisfactory historical explanation: an explanation is satisfactory if and only if it identifies the principal agents whose decisions shaped the outcome, shows how those decisions interacted with the circumstances, and would be accepted as adequate by a competent contemporary observer who possessed all the relevant facts.
>
> But this definition, abstracted from cases of deliberate military action, proves inadequate when applied to one of the most consequential transformations in recorded history: the demographic collapse of indigenous American populations following European contact. The population of the Americas may have fallen by as much as ninety per cent in the century after 1492. The principal cause, as modern scholarship has established, was the introduction of Old World diseases — smallpox, measles, influenza, typhus — against which the indigenous peoples had no immunological defence. This explanation identifies no principal agents whose decisions shaped the outcome. The Spaniards did not deliberately deploy epidemic disease as a weapon; the transmission was largely inadvertent, a biological consequence of contact between populations with different disease histories. Nor would the explanation have been accepted by a competent contemporary observer: no one in the sixteenth century understood the germ theory of disease or the concept of immunological naivety. A contemporary observer, however intelligent, would have attributed the mortality to divine providence, miasma, or the natural constitution of the indigenous peoples. The background cases were all events shaped by identifiable human decisions. The definition mistook agency and contemporary intelligibility for essential features of explanation, when the most important cause of the demographic transformation of the Americas was an impersonal biological process that no one intended, no one controlled, and no one at the time could have understood.

*Abductive insight:* The background cases were all military events in which identifiable commanders made decisions that determined the outcome. The definition abstracted agency and contemporary intelligibility as essential, but the demographic collapse of the Americas was driven by impersonal biological processes that no agent intended and no contemporary could have understood, revealing that some of the most consequential historical explanations invoke causes that are neither agential nor accessible to the understanding of the period.

### 3. periodisation

**Definition:** A periodisation is well-founded if and only if (i) the boundary event is a singular, dateable occurrence, (ii) the character of political and social life differs fundamentally on either side of the boundary, and (iii) contemporaries recognised the event as inaugurating a new era.

> Consider three well-established periodisations. The division of English history at 1066, when the Norman Conquest overthrew the Anglo-Saxon order. The division of French history at 1789, when the Revolution destroyed the ancien régime. The division of Russian history at 1917, when the Bolsheviks seized power. In each case the boundary is a singular, dateable event; the character of life changed fundamentally on either side; and contemporaries were acutely aware that something momentous had occurred. From these cases one might define a well-founded periodisation: it is well-founded if and only if the boundary event is singular and dateable, political and social life differs fundamentally on either side, and contemporaries recognised the event as inaugurating a new era.
>
> But this definition, drawn from dramatic political ruptures, proves too narrow when one considers the periodisation that divides European history into pre-industrial and industrial eras. This division is among the most analytically powerful in modern historical scholarship. It captures a transformation in the material conditions of human life more profound than any political revolution: the shift from an economy based on human and animal muscle, wind, and water to one powered by fossil fuels and machinery. Yet the boundary has no singular, dateable event. The transition to industrial production began in Britain around the 1760s with developments in textile manufacturing and iron production, but it unfolded over decades and spread unevenly across Europe and beyond. Belgium and France industrialised in the early nineteenth century; Germany and the United States followed by mid-century; Russia and Japan came later still. There is no single moment at which the pre-industrial world ended and the industrial world began. Nor did contemporaries recognise the transformation as it occurred. A cotton spinner in Lancashire in 1780 did not suppose himself to be living at the dawn of a new era; he was simply tending his machine. The background cases were all political revolutions whose singularity and drama made them visible to contemporaries. The definition mistook this visibility for an essential property of periodisation, overlooking the slow, diffuse, and impersonal transformations that may be the most consequential divisions in human history.

*Abductive insight:* The background cases — 1066, 1789, 1917 — were all political ruptures: singular events that contemporaries experienced as transformative. The definition abstracted singularity, fundamental difference, and contemporary recognition as essential, but the industrial periodisation reveals that the most analytically important divisions of the past may correspond to diffuse, multi-generational transformations that no single event inaugurated and no contemporary fully recognised.

### 4. civilisation

**Definition:** A civilisation maintains its identity over time if and only if (i) there is institutional continuity between its earlier and later phases, (ii) the language of public life remains the same or develops in an unbroken line, and (iii) the inhabitants regard themselves as belonging to the same civilisation as their ancestors.

> Consider three civilisations celebrated for their continuity. Ancient Egypt, whose dynastic succession sustained a recognisable cultural identity over millennia. China, whose written language, Confucian ethics, and tradition of centralised governance have persisted from antiquity to the present. Rome, whose language, law, and political institutions provided a continuous thread from the city's founding to the fall of the Western Empire. From these cases one might extract a definition: a civilisation maintains its identity over time if and only if there is institutional continuity between its earlier and later phases, the language of public life remains the same or develops in an unbroken line, and the inhabitants regard themselves as belonging to the same civilisation as their ancestors.
>
> But this definition, drawn from civilisations of extraordinary continuity, encounters a severe difficulty when applied to ancient Greece. Historians and philosophers of history routinely speak of Greek civilisation as a continuous entity, extending from the Mycenaean palaces of the second millennium BCE through the Archaic, Classical, and Hellenistic periods. Yet the transition from the Mycenaean to the Archaic age involved a rupture of the most thoroughgoing kind. Around 1200 BCE, Mycenaean palatial civilisation collapsed. The great palaces at Mycenae, Tiryns, and Pylos were destroyed or abandoned. The elaborate bureaucratic institutions that had administered the Mycenaean kingdoms vanished. Literacy was lost: the Linear B script, used for palace records, ceased to be written, and Greece entered a period of several centuries — the so-called Dark Ages — during which no written records were produced. When literacy re-emerged, it took the form of an entirely new script, the Greek alphabet, borrowed from the Phoenicians. There is no evidence that the inhabitants of eighth-century Greece regarded themselves as heirs to the Mycenaean world; the Mycenaean palaces were ruins whose original purpose was forgotten. All three conditions fail for this transition. The background cases were civilisations whose continuity was sustained by persistent institutions, languages, and self-identification. The definition mistook these features for universal requirements of civilisational identity, when Greek civilisation survived a catastrophic break in its institutional, linguistic, and psychological continuity — maintained, perhaps, by deeper and less visible threads: the continuity of settlement, the persistence of material culture, and the eventual recovery, through oral tradition, of the heroic memory of the Mycenaean age.

*Abductive insight:* The background cases — Egypt, China, Rome — were all civilisations characterised by unusual institutional and linguistic continuity. The definition abstracted these features as essential to civilisational identity, but the Greek case reveals that a civilisation may survive a catastrophic interruption in its institutions, its literacy, and its self-understanding, maintaining its identity through deeper continuities of settlement, material culture, and the eventual recovery of its mythological and heroic traditions.

### 5. decline

**Definition:** A civilisation is in decline if and only if (i) it loses control over territories it formerly governed, (ii) it is defeated in military conflicts with rising rival powers, and (iii) its central government becomes progressively weaker and less capable of enforcing its authority.

> Consider three paradigmatic cases of civilisational decline. The Western Roman Empire, which lost its provinces one by one to barbarian peoples, suffered repeated military reverses, and saw its central government dwindle into impotence before the last emperor was deposed in 476. Mughal India, whose later emperors watched helplessly as provincial governors became independent rulers and the empire fragmented. The Spanish Empire of the seventeenth century, which endured military defeats, financial ruin, and the steady loss of its European and colonial possessions. From these cases one might define decline: a civilisation is in decline if and only if it loses control over territories it formerly governed, it is defeated in military conflicts with rising rival powers, and its central government becomes progressively weaker.
>
> But this definition, drawn from cases in which decline was externally visible, proves misleading when applied to the Byzantine Empire under Justinian in the sixth century. Justinian's reign was, on its face, a period of spectacular revival. His generals reconquered North Africa from the Vandals, recovered Italy from the Ostrogoths, and seized parts of southern Spain. The Byzantine army was formidable; the central government in Constantinople was strong, efficient, and ambitious. None of the three conditions of decline obtained. Yet the costs of these reconquests were ruinous. The treasury was drained; the army was overstretched; the provinces were exhausted by taxation. And in 541, the first great plague pandemic struck the empire, killing perhaps a quarter of its population and inflicting a demographic wound from which Byzantium never fully recovered. Within a century of Justinian's death, the reconquered territories were lost — North Africa and Spain to the Arabs, much of Italy to the Lombards. The civilisation had been in decline even during its most triumphant phase, because the triumph was purchased at a cost the empire could not sustain. The background cases were all instances in which decline manifested as visible territorial and military contraction. The definition mistook these symptoms for the essence of decline, overlooking the possibility that a civilisation may be declining most dangerously at the very moment when it appears most powerful, if its expansion conceals the depletion of the foundations upon which its power rests.

*Abductive insight:* The background cases — Rome, Mughal India, Spain — were all instances in which decline was visible in territorial loss, military defeat, and governmental weakness. The definition abstracted these external symptoms as constitutive of decline, but the Byzantine case under Justinian reveals that a civilisation may be in decline even during a period of military triumph and territorial expansion, if the expansion itself is unsustainable and conceals the depletion of the deeper resources — demographic, fiscal, ecological — upon which long-term vitality depends.
