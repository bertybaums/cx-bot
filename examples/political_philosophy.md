## DefCx Examples

Each example presents a definition as necessary and sufficient conditions, then constructs a scenario that reveals the definition's insufficiency.

### 1. freedom

**Definition:** A person is free if and only if (i) he is not subject to physical coercion, (ii) a range of options is available to him, and (iii) he is able to choose among those options.

> It is commonly held that a person is free if and only if he is not subject to physical coercion, a range of options is available to him, and he is able to choose among those options. This definition, however, proves insufficient. Consider a citizen of an authoritarian state who is subjected from birth to pervasive propaganda. He is never physically coerced; indeed, he moves about as he pleases. A range of options is available to him — he may choose his occupation, his residence, his associates. And he is perfectly able to choose among these options. Yet his preferences have been so thoroughly shaped by propaganda that he invariably chooses what the state desires him to choose, and he does so with every appearance of spontaneity. The three conditions are met in full. Yet one feels strongly that this man is not free, for his choices, though uncoerced and unconstrained, are not genuinely his own. The definition has failed to account for the possibility that freedom requires not merely the absence of external constraint but the integrity of the deliberative faculties by which choices are made.

*Missing condition:* The person's preferences and deliberative faculties must not be the product of manipulation or indoctrination.

### 2. democracy

**Definition:** A political system is democratic if and only if (i) political decisions are made by majority vote, (ii) all adult citizens have the right to vote, and (iii) elections are held at regular intervals.

> One might propose that a political system is democratic if and only if political decisions are made by majority vote, all adult citizens have the right to vote, and elections are held at regular intervals. Yet this analysis is too permissive. Consider a state in which elections are held every four years with perfect regularity, in which every adult citizen is entitled to vote, and in which all decisions are taken by majority rule. However, only one political party is permitted to stand for election; all others are banned. The citizens dutifully cast their votes, and the ruling party wins, as it must, by an overwhelming majority. The three conditions are satisfied — there is majority voting, universal suffrage, and regular elections — yet no one would call this arrangement democratic. What is missing is the element of genuine choice: democracy requires not merely the mechanism of voting but the availability of alternatives among which the electorate may choose.

*Missing condition:* There must be genuine competition among candidates or parties, so that the electorate faces a real choice.

### 3. legitimacy

**Definition:** A government is legitimate if and only if (i) it has the consent of the governed, (ii) it rules according to law, and (iii) it protects the fundamental rights of its citizens.

> The view that a government is legitimate if and only if it has the consent of the governed, rules according to law, and protects the fundamental rights of its citizens is attractive in its simplicity. Yet it proves insufficient upon examination. Imagine a colonial power which, upon conquering a territory, conducts a plebiscite among the inhabitants. The people, unfamiliar with the political alternatives and overawed by military force, vote to accept the colonial government. The government then rules meticulously according to its own legal code and protects those rights which it has chosen to enumerate. The three conditions appear to be met: there is consent (expressed by plebiscite), rule of law (the government follows its own statutes), and protection of rights (such as they are). Yet we should be reluctant to call this government legitimate, for the consent was obtained under conditions of ignorance and implicit coercion, and the rights protected are defined by the conqueror rather than by the conquered. The definition has failed to require that consent be genuinely informed and freely given.

*Missing condition:* Consent must be informed and uncoerced, not merely formally expressed.

### 4. authority

**Definition:** A person has authority over others if and only if (i) he occupies a recognised institutional position, (ii) he has the power to issue directives, and (iii) those subject to his directives generally comply.

> It may be suggested that a person has authority over others if and only if he occupies a recognised institutional position, he has the power to issue directives, and those subject to his directives generally comply. But this analysis is vulnerable to the following case. Consider the head of a criminal organisation. He occupies a recognised position within the organisation — his subordinates acknowledge him as their leader. He has the power to issue directives — his word is, within his domain, law. And those subject to his directives generally comply, for the consequences of disobedience are severe. The three conditions are satisfied, yet we should not say that the gangster has authority in any morally significant sense. He has power, certainly, and his power is effective, but authority requires something more: that the institutional position be constituted by morally acceptable rules, and that compliance be grounded in recognition of the legitimacy of those rules rather than in mere fear of punishment.

*Missing condition:* The institutional position must be constituted by morally acceptable rules, not merely by effective power.

### 5. equality

**Definition:** Citizens are treated equally if and only if (i) the same laws apply to all, (ii) no person receives preferential treatment from the state, and (iii) all have equal access to public institutions.

> One is inclined to say that citizens are treated equally if and only if the same laws apply to all, no person receives preferential treatment from the state, and all have equal access to public institutions. Yet this purely formal conception of equality proves insufficient. Consider a law which prohibits sleeping beneath the bridges of Paris. This law applies to all citizens without distinction: the rich man and the poor man are alike forbidden to sleep under bridges. No one receives preferential treatment; the law is enforced impartially. And all citizens have equal access to the courts should they wish to challenge the law. The three conditions are satisfied. Yet there is something deeply unequal about the arrangement, for the law burdens only the poor, since only they have occasion to sleep under bridges. The rich man's formal equality with the poor man is, in this instance, a mockery. The definition has failed to account for the substantive dimension of equality: that formally identical treatment may produce grossly unequal effects when applied to persons in materially different circumstances.

*Missing condition:* Equality requires attention to the differential impact of formally equal rules upon persons in different circumstances.

## AbdCx Examples

Each example presents background cases that motivate a definition, then constructs a scenario revealing what the background cases shared but the definition missed.

### 1. civil disobedience

**Definition:** An act is one of civil disobedience if and only if (i) it is a deliberate violation of law, (ii) it is motivated by moral or political conviction, and (iii) the agent accepts the legal consequences of his action.

> Consider the following cases of civil disobedience. A group of citizens peacefully refuses to pay a tax they regard as unjust, accepting the legal penalties. Protesters occupy a public building to draw attention to racial segregation, submitting willingly to arrest. A conscientious objector publicly refuses military service and presents himself for trial. From these cases one might extract a definition: an act is one of civil disobedience if and only if it is a deliberate violation of law, it is motivated by moral or political conviction, and the agent accepts the legal consequences of his action.
>
> Yet this definition admits cases that do not seem to be civil disobedience at all. Suppose a factory owner sincerely believes that environmental regulations are unjust and deliberately violates them, willingly paying the resulting fines. He acts from moral conviction, violates the law deliberately, and accepts the consequences. The three conditions are met. Yet this is not civil disobedience; it is a private act of non-compliance undertaken for personal advantage under the cover of moral language. The background cases were all public, communicative acts — they were addressed to the conscience of the community, seeking to persuade fellow citizens of an injustice. The definition failed to capture this essential publicity and communicative purpose, admitting merely private law-breaking alongside genuinely political acts of protest.

*Abductive insight:* The background cases were all public, communicative acts aimed at persuading fellow citizens. The definition dropped the publicity and communicative dimensions, admitting private acts of law-breaking that lack the political character of civil disobedience.

### 2. sovereignty

**Definition:** A state is sovereign if and only if (i) it exercises supreme authority within its territory, (ii) it commands a monopoly on the legitimate use of force, and (iii) it is recognised by other states.

> Consider the following cases of sovereignty. France exercises supreme authority within its borders, controls its military, and is recognised by other states as sovereign. The United Kingdom makes its own laws, commands its armed forces, and maintains diplomatic relations worldwide. Japan governs its territory independently, has a national defence force, and is a member of international organisations. From these cases one might extract a definition: a state is sovereign if and only if it exercises supreme authority within its territory, commands a monopoly on the legitimate use of force, and is recognised by other states.
>
> But consider a state which is recognised by the international community, which claims supreme authority and a nominal monopoly on force, yet which has in practice lost control of large portions of its territory to armed insurgent groups. The government issues decrees that are not enforced, collects no taxes outside the capital, and exercises no authority over the rebel-held regions. The three conditions appear nominally to be met — the state claims authority, claims a monopoly on force, and enjoys international recognition — yet it is not sovereign in any meaningful sense over much of its territory. The background cases all involved states with effective control throughout their territory; the definition failed to distinguish between the claim to authority and its effective exercise.

*Abductive insight:* The background cases involved states with effective control throughout their territory. The definition did not distinguish between claiming authority and effectively exercising it, admitting states whose sovereignty is nominal rather than actual.

### 3. toleration

**Definition:** A political arrangement constitutes toleration if and only if (i) the state permits practices it has the power to suppress, (ii) the permitted practices are ones the state or the dominant group regards as wrong or undesirable, and (iii) the permission is stable and not subject to arbitrary revocation.

> Consider the following cases of toleration. The Edict of Nantes granted French Protestants the right to worship freely, and the resulting coexistence was widely regarded as a form of toleration. In the Dutch Republic, Catholics, Jews, and various Protestant sects coexisted, each permitted to practise its faith, and the arrangement was celebrated as a model of toleration. After the Toleration Act of 1689, English Dissenters were permitted to worship outside the established church, and this too was called toleration. From these cases one might extract a definition: a political arrangement constitutes toleration if and only if the state permits practices it has the power to suppress, the permitted practices are ones the state or the dominant group regards as wrong or undesirable, and the permission is stable and not subject to arbitrary revocation.
>
> Yet this analysis admits of a troublesome case. Consider a colonial power which permits the religious practices of its subject peoples. The coloniser regards these practices as primitive and inferior. It has the military and administrative apparatus to suppress them. The permission is stable, sustained over decades as a matter of settled policy. The three conditions are satisfied. Yet the arrangement seems less like toleration than like imperial condescension. The coloniser does not permit the practices because it recognises any claim the subject peoples have to practise them; it permits them because suppression would provoke rebellion and disrupt the extraction of resources. The background cases — the Edict of Nantes, the Dutch Republic, the Toleration Act — all involved, however imperfectly, a recognition that the tolerated groups had some principled claim to practise as they did. The definition captured the structural features of these arrangements but dropped this normative dimension, admitting cases of mere pragmatic forbearance alongside genuine toleration.

*Abductive insight:* The background cases all involved a degree of principled recognition — however reluctant — that the tolerated groups had some claim to practise as they did. The definition captured the structural features of these arrangements but dropped the normative dimension: that genuine toleration involves a principled reason for forbearance, not merely a pragmatic one.

### 4. representation

**Definition:** A person represents a group if and only if (i) he has been authorised by the group to act on its behalf, (ii) his actions are binding upon the group, and (iii) the group has a mechanism for holding him to account.

> Consider three cases of representation. A member of Parliament elected by the citizens of a constituency speaks on their behalf in the House; we say he represents them. A trade union delegate chosen by the workers of a factory negotiates with management on their behalf; he too represents them. An ambassador appointed by his government speaks for his nation in foreign courts; he represents the nation. From these cases one might extract a definition: a person represents a group if and only if he has been authorised by the group to act on its behalf, his actions are binding upon the group, and the group has a mechanism for holding him to account.
>
> But consider a case which tells against this analysis. A protest movement arises spontaneously in a large city. It has no formal structure, no elected leaders, and no constitution. Yet one man, by virtue of his eloquence and his intimate understanding of the movement's grievances, begins to speak to the press on its behalf. He has received no authorisation; his statements are not formally binding upon the movement; and there is no mechanism by which the participants might hold him to account. None of the three conditions is satisfied. Yet the members of the movement recognise his statements as faithful expressions of their views, and the public comes to understand the movement's aims through his words. He represents the movement in a substantive sense. The background cases were all instances of formal, institutional representation — elections, appointments, mandates. The definition captured what these cases had in common but missed the possibility that representation may arise informally, through the faithful articulation of a group's views, without any institutional apparatus whatever.

*Abductive insight:* The background cases all involved formal institutions of authorisation — elections, appointments, mandates. The definition captured the institutional trappings of representation but missed the possibility of substantive representation without formal authorisation, where a person represents a group by virtue of faithfully articulating its views and interests.

### 5. political obligation

**Definition:** A person has a political obligation to obey a rule if and only if (i) he has voluntarily accepted benefits from the institution that enacted the rule, (ii) the institution is reasonably just, and (iii) the rule was enacted through fair procedures.

> Consider three cases in which political obligation seems clear. A citizen who has voted in elections, used public roads, and been protected by the police is said to have an obligation to obey the laws of his state. A man who has accepted a government scholarship and pledged to serve afterwards is said to owe compliance with the terms of his pledge. Members of a cooperative who have contributed labour and shared the harvest are said to owe one another adherence to the cooperative's rules. From these cases one might extract a definition: a person has a political obligation to obey a rule if and only if he has voluntarily accepted benefits from the institution that enacted the rule, the institution is reasonably just, and the rule was enacted through fair procedures.
>
> Yet this analysis encounters difficulty with the following case. A hermit lives on remote public land, far from any settlement. He has never voted, never used a public road, and has declined every service the state has offered. He grows his own food, draws his own water, and builds his own shelter. He has not voluntarily accepted any benefit from the state. A new law requires all residents to register their dwellings with the local authority. The first condition of our definition fails: the hermit has accepted no benefits. Yet there is some force to the argument that he is nevertheless obligated to comply, for he occupies territory over which the state exercises jurisdiction, and his very security — his freedom from invasion and assault — depends, whether he acknowledges it or not, upon the state's existence. The background cases were all instances of clear, voluntary participation in cooperative schemes. The definition abstracted a principle of reciprocity from these cases but could not accommodate the obligation that may attach to mere residence within a jurisdiction, independently of any voluntary act of acceptance.

*Abductive insight:* The background cases all involved persons who had clearly participated in and benefited from cooperative arrangements. The definition generalised from these cases a principle of reciprocity, but it failed to account for the obligation that may arise from mere residence within a jurisdiction, independently of any voluntary acceptance of benefits.
