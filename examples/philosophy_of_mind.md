## DefCx Examples

Each example presents a definition as necessary and sufficient conditions, then constructs a scenario that reveals the definition's insufficiency.

### 1. intention

**Definition:** A person intends to perform an action if and only if (i) he desires a certain outcome, (ii) he believes the action will produce that outcome, and (iii) he has formed a plan to carry out the action.

> One might define intention as follows: a person intends to perform an action if and only if he desires a certain outcome, believes the action will produce that outcome, and has formed a plan to carry out the action. This analysis, however, is shown to be insufficient by a familiar sort of case. Consider a man who sincerely desires to lose weight. He believes, correctly, that running each morning will produce this outcome. And he has formed a detailed plan: he will rise at six, don his running clothes, and jog for thirty minutes along a specified route. He has the desire, the belief, and the plan. Yet each morning, when the alarm sounds, he remains in bed. He never runs. The three conditions are met, but we should not say that the man intends to run, for intention involves something more than the conjunction of desire, belief, and plan. It requires that the agent have settled the matter, that he be genuinely disposed to execute the plan rather than merely to contemplate it. The definition has confused idle resolution with genuine intention.

*Missing condition:* The agent must have settled the matter in his own mind and be disposed to act on the plan, not merely to entertain it.

### 2. action

**Definition:** An event is an action if and only if (i) it is a bodily movement, (ii) it is caused by the agent's desire and belief, and (iii) the agent is aware of performing it.

> The causal theory of action holds that an event is an action if and only if it is a bodily movement, it is caused by the agent's desire and belief, and the agent is aware of performing it. Yet this theory is susceptible to a well-known difficulty. Suppose a nervous lecturer desires to appear calm before his audience and believes that holding his hands steady will produce this impression. But his very anxiety about his desire to appear calm causes his hands to tremble. The trembling is a bodily movement; it is caused, in a perfectly straightforward sense, by the agent's desire and belief — for it is his desire to appear calm and his belief about the importance of steady hands that have produced the anxiety that causes the trembling. And the agent is painfully aware of the movement. All three conditions are satisfied. Yet the trembling is not an action; it is something that happens to the agent, not something he does. The definition has failed to specify that the causal connection between desire and movement must proceed through the rational capacities of the agent, not through deviant causal pathways such as anxiety.

*Missing condition:* The desire and belief must cause the bodily movement in the right way, through the normal exercise of the agent's rational capacities.

### 3. emotion

**Definition:** A mental state is an emotion if and only if (i) it involves a feeling of a characteristic quality, (ii) it is directed towards an object, and (iii) it disposes the subject to characteristic behaviour.

> A commonly offered analysis holds that a mental state is an emotion if and only if it involves a feeling of a characteristic quality, it is directed towards an object, and it disposes the subject to characteristic behaviour. Yet one may construct a case which calls this analysis into question. Suppose a man ingests a pharmaceutical compound which produces in him a state phenomenologically indistinguishable from intense anger. He feels the hot flush, the tightness in the chest, the clenching of the fists. His state is, let us say, directed towards his neighbour, who happens to be present. And he is disposed to characteristic behaviour — he feels the urge to shout and strike. The three conditions are satisfied. Yet we should hesitate to call this state an emotion in the proper sense, for it bears no rational connection to its object. The man has no reason to be angry at his neighbour; the feeling is entirely the product of a chemical. The definition has failed to capture the requirement that genuine emotion involves an appraisal or evaluation of its object.

*Missing condition:* The feeling must arise from an appraisal or evaluation of the object, not from a purely physiological cause unconnected with the object.

### 4. consciousness

**Definition:** A being is conscious if and only if (i) it receives and processes information from its environment, (ii) it responds differentially to distinct stimuli, and (iii) it modifies its behaviour in light of past experience.

> It is tempting to define consciousness in functional terms: a being is conscious if and only if it receives and processes information from its environment, responds differentially to distinct stimuli, and modifies its behaviour in light of past experience. This definition, however, proves far too permissive. Consider a common thermostat. It receives information about the temperature of its surroundings through a bimetallic strip or electronic sensor; it processes this information, comparing the current temperature to a set point; it responds differentially, activating a furnace when the temperature is low and remaining quiescent when it is high; and a modern thermostat may modify its behaviour based on past patterns, learning when the household is likely to require heat. The three conditions are satisfied. Yet we should be most reluctant to attribute consciousness to a thermostat. What is missing from the definition is the subjective dimension: a conscious being is one for which there is something it is like to be that being, and no amount of information-processing and differential response guarantees the presence of such inner experience.

*Missing condition:* The being must have subjective experience — there must be something it is like to be that being.

### 5. personal identity

**Definition:** A person at time t2 is identical to a person at time t1 if and only if (i) there is bodily continuity between them, (ii) there is continuity of memory, and (iii) there is continuity of character and personality.

> It is often proposed that a person at one time is identical to a person at an earlier time if and only if there is bodily continuity between them, there is continuity of memory, and there is continuity of character and personality. But this analysis encounters difficulties in both directions. Consider a man who suffers a severe blow to the head and loses all memory of his previous life. His character, too, is transformed: where before he was gentle and retiring, he is now bold and sociable. Yet his body is manifestly continuous — it is the same organism that entered and left the hospital. If all three conditions are required for identity, we must deny that the amnesiac is the same person as the man who was struck, which seems absurd: his wife, his doctor, and the law all treat him as the same person. One of the three conditions — bodily continuity — is met, while the other two fail entirely. The definition, it appears, is too demanding: it requires the conjunction of conditions which, taken singly, may each be sufficient for identity.

*Missing condition:* The conditions may be individually sufficient but not jointly necessary; personal identity may admit of degrees rather than requiring all three conditions simultaneously.

## AbdCx Examples

Each example presents background cases that motivate a definition, then constructs a scenario revealing what the background cases shared but the definition missed.

### 1. free will

**Definition:** An action is free if and only if (i) the agent deliberates before acting, (ii) the agent acts on his own desires, and (iii) no external force compels the action.

> Consider three cases in which we readily ascribe freedom. A man deliberates carefully and chooses to accept a job offer; we say he acts freely. A woman weighs the arguments and votes for the candidate she judges best; this too seems free. A student considers his options and decides to study medicine; again, a free choice. From these cases one might extract the following definition: an action is free if and only if the agent deliberates before acting, acts on his own desires, and no external force compels the action.
>
> Yet this definition proves too permissive. Consider a man who suffers from an irresistible compulsion to wash his hands. He deliberates — he knows the compulsion is irrational and considers resisting it. He acts on his own desire, for the compulsion is genuinely his; it is not imposed by another person. And no external force compels him. The three conditions are met. Yet we should not say he acts freely, for his action is driven by a compulsion he cannot overcome by any effort of will. The background cases all involved agents whose desires were ones they could reflectively endorse — desires that were, as it were, truly their own. The definition failed to distinguish between desires the agent identifies with and desires that grip him against his better judgement.

*Abductive insight:* The background cases involved agents whose desires were ones they reflectively endorsed. The definition failed to distinguish between desires the agent identifies with and desires that grip him against his reflective judgement.

### 2. perception

**Definition:** A person perceives an object if and only if (i) the object causally affects his sense organs, (ii) he forms a mental representation of the object, and (iii) the representation is veridical.

> Consider three straightforward cases of perception. A man sees a red apple on the table before him and correctly identifies it as a red apple. A woman hears a church bell ringing and correctly identifies the sound. A child touches a hot stove and immediately perceives the heat. From these cases one might define perception: a person perceives an object if and only if the object causally affects his sense organs, he forms a mental representation of the object, and the representation is veridical — that is, it accurately represents the object.
>
> But consider a man who looks at a white wall illuminated by a red light. The wall causally affects his eyes; he forms a mental representation — of a red wall. Is the representation veridical? In one sense, yes: the wall does appear red under the prevailing conditions, and the man's visual system is functioning correctly. In another sense, no: the wall is white, and the man perceives it as red. The background cases all involved perception under standard conditions — normal lighting, unobstructed view, functioning senses. The definition failed to specify what counts as veridical representation when conditions are non-standard, and accordingly cannot distinguish between perceiving an object as it is and perceiving it as conditions make it appear.

*Abductive insight:* The background cases all involved objects perceived under standard conditions. The definition did not specify what counts as a veridical representation when conditions are non-standard, conflating the appearance of the object under prevailing conditions with its intrinsic properties.

### 3. self deception

**Definition:** A person is self-deceived about p if and only if (i) p is false, (ii) the person holds p to be true, and (iii) the person has access to sufficient evidence that p is false.

> Consider three cases of self-deception. A woman whose husband is plainly unfaithful insists, against all evidence, that he is devoted to her; we say she is self-deceived. A gambler who has lost a fortune persists in the conviction that his luck is about to turn, despite overwhelming statistical evidence to the contrary; he too is self-deceived. A man diagnosed with a serious illness insists he is perfectly well, ignoring the testimony of multiple physicians; again, self-deception. From these cases one might extract a definition: a person is self-deceived about p if and only if p is false, the person holds p to be true, and the person has access to sufficient evidence that p is false.
>
> Yet the analysis encounters a subtle difficulty. Suppose a man holds the proposition that his colleagues like him. He has access to what appears to be evidence to the contrary: his colleagues whisper behind his back, greet him coolly, and exclude him from social gatherings. Yet in fact his colleagues do like him; the apparent evidence of hostility arises from their natural reserve and cultural reticence. The proposition p — that his colleagues like him — is true. The first condition of the definition fails. Yet the man's grounds for holding p are not the evidence at all; he holds p because of a desperate need to be liked, and he would hold p just as tenaciously if his colleagues truly despised him. His belief-forming process is precisely that of the self-deceived person: he ignores apparent counter-evidence and clings to a comforting proposition for motivational reasons. The background cases all involved false propositions, and the definition required falsity. But self-deception, one suspects, is better characterised by the irrational, motivationally-driven process of belief-formation than by the truth-value of the resulting proposition.

*Abductive insight:* The background cases all involved false propositions held true against the evidence. The definition required the falsity of p, but self-deception may be characterised more by the irrational process of belief-formation than by the truth-value of the proposition believed.

### 4. attention

**Definition:** A subject attends to X if and only if (i) he selects X from a field of competing stimuli, (ii) he sustains focus on X over some interval of time, and (iii) his processing of X is enhanced relative to unselected stimuli.

> Consider three cases of attention. A student concentrates on a difficult passage in a textbook, blocking out the noise of the common room; we say he is attending to the text. A birdwatcher scans a hedgerow, singling out a rare species from the foliage; she is attending to the bird. A pianist practising a difficult passage focuses on the coordination of her fingers; she is attending to the fingering. From these cases one might define attention: a subject attends to X if and only if he selects X from a field of competing stimuli, sustains focus on X over some interval, and his processing of X is enhanced relative to unselected stimuli.
>
> But this definition, drawn from cases of voluntary, goal-directed attention, fails to accommodate a familiar phenomenon. Suppose a man is walking down a quiet street, lost in thought. A loud explosion occurs nearby. In an instant, his awareness is wholly captured by the sound. He did not select the explosion from a field of competing stimuli — on the contrary, the explosion overwhelmed all other stimuli and seized his awareness without any act of selection on his part. Nor did he sustain focus over any interval, for the sound was over almost as soon as it began. Yet in that brief moment he was undeniably attending to the explosion — attending to it with an intensity that surpassed anything in the background cases. The definition captured the selective, sustained, goal-directed character of the background cases but missed the phenomenon of involuntary attentional capture, in which a sufficiently salient stimulus seizes awareness without selection, without sustained focus, and without any contribution from the subject's goals.

*Abductive insight:* The background cases were all instances of voluntary, sustained attention directed by the subject's goals. The definition captured the selective and sustained character of these cases but failed to accommodate involuntary, transient attention — the capture of awareness by a sudden, salient stimulus.

### 5. dreaming

**Definition:** A mental episode is a dream if and only if (i) it occurs during sleep, (ii) it presents a narrative or sequence of events, and (iii) the events are experienced as if they were perceived rather than merely thought.

> Consider three cases of dreaming. A man dreams that he is being chased through a dark forest and wakes in terror; we call this a dream. A woman dreams she is flying over a city, experiencing vivid sensations of wind and height; this too is a dream. A child dreams that he is at school without his clothes and wakes embarrassed; again, a dream. From these cases one might extract a definition: a mental episode is a dream if and only if it occurs during sleep, presents a narrative or sequence of events, and the events are experienced as if perceived rather than merely thought.
>
> But this definition, drawn from canonical cases of extended, narrative dreams, proves too narrow. Consider a person in the early stages of sleep who experiences a single, isolated image: a vivid flash of a face, brilliant and unbidden, with no narrative context and no sequence of events. The image appears and vanishes. It is experienced not as a thought but as a perception — it has the vividness and the involuntary character of something seen. The first and third conditions are met: the episode occurs during sleep and is experienced as perceived. But the second condition fails, for there is no narrative and no sequence of events. Yet these hypnagogic images are commonly described as dreams, or at the very least as phenomena continuous with dreaming, and they share the essential phenomenal character of the dream: involuntary, vivid, perception-like experience during sleep. The background cases were all extended narrative dreams, and the definition captured their narrative structure. But it excluded the fragmentary, imagistic episodes at the threshold of sleep, which belong, one suspects, to the same family of phenomena.

*Abductive insight:* The background cases were all extended, narrative dreams of the sort most commonly reported upon waking. The definition captured the narrative structure of these canonical examples but excluded the fragmentary, non-narrative dream images that occur at the threshold of sleep, which share the phenomenological character of dreaming without its narrative form.
